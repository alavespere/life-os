# Snapshot: Insurance Claims and Coverage in USA

**Source:** Sullivan & Cromwell LLP | **Date:** April 17, 2026

A comprehensive multi-state reference on US insurance claims and coverage law. Key sections:

## Third-Party Direct Actions
- Most states require an unsatisfied judgment before a third party can sue an insurer directly
- Louisiana narrowed its long-standing direct action statute as of August 2024 — now only allows direct actions in very limited circumstances (insolvency, unreachable insured, deceased insured)
- New York allows direct actions when judgment is unsatisfied (NY Ins Law 3420)
- Assignments of rights to third parties are generally permitted; some states void anti-assignment clauses once a loss occurs

## Late Notice of Claim
- Occurrence-based policies: most states require the insurer to show **prejudice** before denying coverage for late notice
- Claims-made-and-reported policies: courts are far more likely to deny coverage for late notice regardless of prejudice — notification is an express part of the insuring agreement
- New York reformed its rule in 2009 — now requires prejudice showing for liability policies

## Bad Faith / Wrongful Denial
- Most jurisdictions allow extra-contractual damages when insurer acts in bad faith (failure to settle within limits, wrongful denial of defense/indemnity)
- Standard varies by state: from "failure to act reasonably" to "wilful misconduct"
- Florida: affirmative duty to settle even without a demand when liability is clear and excess judgment is probable
- Some states (NY) prohibit bad faith practices by statute but provide no private right of action

## Duty to Defend
- Triggered by the "four corners" rule in most jurisdictions — compare complaint allegations to policy provisions
- Duty to defend is broader than duty to indemnify
- If even one claim in a complaint could be covered, insurer typically must defend the entire action
- Texas recently opened the door to extrinsic evidence in narrow circumstances

## Excess Insurer Obligations
- Whether excess insurer must "drop down" if primary is insolvent is a matter of contract language
- Absent express drop-down language, courts split — some enforce literal exhaustion requirement, others apply public policy rationale
- New Jersey statute: settling primary layer deemed exhausted for triggering excess layer

## Self-Insured Retentions (SIRs)
- Two schools: (1) public policy — insurer still responsible above SIR even if insured insolvent; (2) strict contract — SIR payment is condition precedent regardless of insolvency
- Deductibles within policy limits: insurer generally must pay, then seek reimbursement from insolvent insured

## Occurrence Definition
- Policy language governs whether multiple injuries = one or multiple occurrences
- "Cause" test vs. "unfortunate event" test — courts split
- New York applies the "unfortunate event" test (each injury may be a separate occurrence)

## Rescission for Misrepresentation
- Requires material misstatement that the insurer relied on in issuing the policy
- Some states also require intent to defraud
- Fidelity insurance treated differently — dishonest employee's knowledge not imputed to insured for rescission purposes

## Relevance to BuildSafe / Brokerage
- Late notice rules are critical for contractor claims — especially on occurrence-based CGL policies
- Louisiana's narrowed direct action statute matters for any Gulf Coast placements
- SIR vs. deductible distinction is a practical underwriting issue for commercial GC accounts
- Bad faith exposure varies significantly by state — important for any E&O considerations
