## Executive Summary

The failure of a subcontractor to perform can be one of the most financially damaging events a General Contractor (GC) can experience. Delays, cost overruns, defective work, lien claims, and cascading project failures are all direct consequences of engaging an unqualified specialty trade contractor. The Construction Financial Management Association (CFMA), the Associated General Contractors of America (AGC), the American Institute of Architects (AIA), and major surety and insurance underwriters have collectively developed a rigorous, multi-dimensional framework for evaluating subcontractor qualifications before any contract is awarded.

This document provides an exhaustive, no-holds-barred analysis of every data point used to determine whether a subcontractor is qualified to work a job. The evaluation framework is organized around the foundational **"3 Cs of Prequalification"** — **Capacity, Capital, and Character** — which mirror the underwriting criteria used by surety bond companies [1]. Layered on top of this framework are insurance risk considerations, operational assessments, legal compliance reviews, and advanced risk scoring methodologies used in Subcontractor Default Insurance (SDI) programs.

---

## Part I: The Framework — The 3 Cs of Prequalification

The CFMA explicitly aligns subcontractor prequalification with surety bond underwriting. As stated in CFMA's published guidance: *"Essentially, subcontractor prequalification criteria are akin to that of the surety bond underwriting process. To determine whether to prequalify a subcontractor, contractors should gather information from each of the three 'Cs' — capacity, capital, and character — in order to gain insight into the overall capabilities of the subcontractor"* [1].

This framework is not merely theoretical. It is the operational backbone of every serious prequalification program in the commercial construction industry, from large national GCs to regional specialty contractors.

## Part II: Capital — Financial Stability and Benchmarks

Financial health is the most quantitatively measurable dimension of prequalification. It determines whether a subcontractor can mobilize, sustain operations through negative cash flow cycles, pay its downstream suppliers and labor, and survive unexpected project challenges without defaulting. Financial prequalification requires the collection and analysis of formal financial statements, Work in Progress (WIP) schedules, and credit reports.

### 2.1 Required Financial Documents

The level of financial documentation required scales with the contract value. The following hierarchy is standard across the industry:

| Contract Value Threshold | Required Financial Statement Level |
| :--- | :--- |
| Under $500,000 | Company-prepared financial statements (Balance Sheet, Income Statement) |
| $500,000 – $2,000,000 | CPA-reviewed financial statements (SSARS Review) |
| $2,000,000 – $5,000,000 | CPA-reviewed or audited financial statements |
| Over $5,000,000 | Fully audited financial statements (GAAS Audit) |

In addition to financial statements, GCs should collect the following supporting documents:
- **Work in Progress (WIP) Schedule** with contract values, costs incurred, estimated costs to complete, billings to date, and percentage complete for all active and recently completed contracts.
- **Accounts Receivable (A/R) Aging Schedule** broken down by 0–30, 31–60, 61–90, and 90+ days.
- **Accounts Payable (A/P) Aging Schedule** to assess payment practices to suppliers and sub-subcontractors.
- **Bank Line of Credit Confirmation Letter** indicating the available credit facility and current outstanding balance.
- **Most Recent Interim Financial Statements** (within 90 days) for large contracts.

### 2.2 CFMA Financial Benchmarks — Liquidity Ratios

Liquidity ratios measure the subcontractor's ability to meet short-term financial obligations. The CFMA's *2024 Construction Financial Benchmarker* provides the following industry benchmarks, segmented for Specialty Trade contractors (which represent 87.4% of subcontractors) [2]:

| Liquidity Metric | Formula | CFMA 2023 All Companies | CFMA 2023 Specialty Trade | Best in Class | Red Flag Threshold |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Current Ratio** | Current Assets / Current Liabilities | 1.6 | ~1.6 | >2.0 | < 1.1 |
| **Quick Ratio (Acid Test)** | (Cash + Securities + A/R) / Current Liabilities | 1.4 | ~1.4 | >1.8 | < 1.0 |
| **Days of Cash on Hand** | Cash / ((Oper. Exp. – Depreciation) / 365) | 23.5 Days | ~22 Days | >40 Days | < 10 Days |
| **Working Capital Compression** | Working Capital / Current Assets | — | — | >0.35 | < 0.15 |
| **Underbillings to Working Capital** | Underbillings / Working Capital | 8.0% | ~8% | < 5% | > 25% |

**Current Ratio** is the most widely cited liquidity metric. A ratio of 1.6 means the firm has $1.60 in current assets for every $1.00 of current liabilities — a healthy buffer. A ratio below 1.1 signals that the firm is operating with almost no liquidity cushion and is at high risk of being unable to pay its bills if even one project experiences a payment delay.

**Days of Cash on Hand** is particularly important in construction, where payment cycles are notoriously slow. The CFMA benchmark of 23.5 days means the average firm can only operate for approximately three weeks without new cash inflows. A GC awarding a large contract to a firm with fewer than 10 days of cash is essentially providing an unsecured line of credit.

**Underbillings to Working Capital** is a construction-specific metric that reveals hidden risk. Underbillings represent work performed but not yet invoiced — they are essentially an unsecured receivable. When underbillings represent a large percentage of working capital, the firm's liquidity is illusory, dependent on future billings that may be disputed or delayed [3].

### 2.3 CFMA Financial Benchmarks — Profitability Ratios

Profitability ratios reveal the subcontractor's ability to generate earnings from its operations, which is the ultimate source of equity growth and financial resilience.

| Profitability Metric | Formula | CFMA 2023 All Companies | CFMA 2023 Specialty Trade | Best in Class |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Gross Profit Margin** | (Revenue – Cost of Sales) / Revenue | ~14.8% | ~15–16% | 21.8% |
| **Net Profit Margin (Pre-Tax)** | Net Income Before Tax / Revenue | 6.3% | 6.9% | 11.9% |
| **Return on Assets (ROA)** | Net Income / Total Assets | 11.8% | 13.4% | 28.4% |
| **Return on Equity (ROE)** | Net Income / Total Equity | 31.4% | 31.4% | 59.7% |
| **Revenue per FTE** | Total Revenue / Full-Time Equivalent Employees | $450,086 | — | — |
| **Gross Profit per FTE** | Gross Profit / Full-Time Equivalent Employees | $70,642 | — | — |

The CFMA's "Best in Class" designation represents the top 25% of performers. These firms achieved a **28.4% ROA and 59.7% ROE** in 2023, compared to the all-company averages of 11.8% and 31.4% respectively [2]. A GC evaluating a subcontractor should compare these ratios to the CFMA benchmarks and understand where the sub falls in the performance distribution.

A **Net Profit Margin below 2%** is a significant warning sign. It indicates the firm is operating on razor-thin margins with virtually no buffer to absorb cost overruns, change order disputes, or payment delays. Firms in this position are at elevated risk of "job borrowing" — using cash from profitable projects to fund losses on others — which is a precursor to default.

### 2.4 CFMA Financial Benchmarks — Leverage and Debt Ratios

Leverage ratios measure the extent to which the subcontractor is financed by debt versus equity. Highly leveraged firms are more vulnerable to economic downturns and interest rate increases.

| Leverage Metric | Formula | CFMA 2023 All Companies | Best in Class | Red Flag Threshold |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Debt to Equity Ratio** | Total Liabilities / Total Equity | 1.3x | 0.9x | > 3.0x |
| **Debt (Less Cash) to Equity** | (Total Liabilities – Cash) / Total Equity | — | — | > 2.5x |
| **Times Interest Earned** | EBIT / Interest Expense | 25.6x | — | < 5.0x |
| **Fixed Asset Ratio** | Fixed Assets / Total Assets | 23.7% | 12.9% | > 50% |
| **Equity to SG&A Expense** | Total Equity / SG&A Expense | 1.9x | — | < 0.8x |
| **Revenue to Equity** | Total Revenue / Total Equity | 5.4x | — | > 15x |

The **Best in Class firms reported a Debt to Equity ratio of only 0.9x**, compared to the industry average of 1.3x [2]. This indicates that top performers maintain a more conservative capital structure, which provides greater resilience during project execution challenges.

The **Fixed Asset Ratio** of 12.9% for Best in Class firms versus 23.7% for all companies is also telling. Firms with a high proportion of fixed assets (heavy equipment, real estate) may have less liquid balance sheets and could be over-invested in depreciating assets relative to their working capital.

### 2.5 CFMA Financial Benchmarks — Efficiency and Backlog Ratios

Efficiency ratios measure how well the subcontractor manages its operational cycle, while backlog ratios provide insight into future revenue and capacity utilization.

| Efficiency/Backlog Metric | Formula | CFMA 2023 All Companies |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Days in Accounts Receivable** | (A/R / Revenue) x 365 | 56.6 Days |
| **Days in Accounts Payable** | (A/P / Revenue) x 365 | 33.0 Days |
| **Days in Inventory** | (Inventory / Revenue) x 365 | 6.4 Days |
| **Operating Cycle** | Days A/R + Days Inventory – Days A/P | 52.7 Days |
| **Asset Turnover Ratio** | Revenue / Total Assets | 2.3x |
| **Working Capital Turnover** | Revenue / Working Capital | 7.4x |
| **Accounts Receivable Turnover** | Revenue / Accounts Receivable | ~6.5x |
| **Months in Backlog** | (Backlog / Annual Revenue) x 12 | 8.7 Months |
| **Backlog to Working Capital** | Backlog / Working Capital | 5.9x |
| **Average Backlog to Equity** | Average Backlog / Total Equity | 4.4x |

**Days in Accounts Receivable (56.6 days)** reveals that the average construction firm waits nearly two months to collect payment. A subcontractor with A/R Days significantly above this benchmark may be experiencing collection problems or working with slow-paying GCs, which strains cash flow.

**Months in Backlog (8.7 months)** indicates the average firm has approximately 8.7 months of future work under contract. A backlog below 3 months suggests the firm may be desperate for work and willing to underbid, while a backlog exceeding 18 months may indicate the firm is overextended and cannot staff the proposed project adequately.

**Backlog to Working Capital (5.9x)** is a critical risk indicator. If a firm's backlog is 10x or more its working capital, it is attempting to execute far more work than its financial resources can support, creating a high probability of cash flow failure [3].

### 2.6 Work in Progress (WIP) Schedule Deep Analysis

The WIP schedule is the most important document in subcontractor financial prequalification. It is a real-time snapshot of the firm's project portfolio and reveals financial risks that do not appear on standard financial statements. Key data points extracted from the WIP include:

**For each active project, the WIP should disclose:**
- Contract value (including approved change orders)
- Revised estimated cost at completion
- Costs incurred to date
- Estimated cost to complete
- Percentage complete (cost method)
- Billings to date
- Overbillings (Billings in Excess of Costs and Estimated Earnings)
- Underbillings (Costs and Estimated Earnings in Excess of Billings)
- Estimated gross profit at completion
- Original estimated gross profit at bid
- Profit fade (variance between original and current estimated gross profit)

**Key WIP Analysis Metrics:**

*Profit Fade:* Consistent downward revisions in estimated gross profit across multiple projects is the single most reliable predictor of subcontractor financial distress. It indicates that the firm is either a poor estimator or is experiencing field execution problems that are eroding margins project by project.

*Underbillings as a Percentage of Working Capital:* As noted in the CFMA benchmarks, underbillings should be less than 8–10% of working capital. High underbillings may represent unapproved change orders (a collection risk), billing delays, or disguised project losses.

*Cash to Overbillings Ratio:* This ratio measures whether the cash collected from overbillings has been retained or spent. A low ratio suggests the firm has been using overbilled cash to fund operations or other projects — a classic sign of "job borrowing" [3].

## Part III: Capacity — Operational and Technical Evaluation

Capacity assessment determines whether the subcontractor has the operational infrastructure, human capital, equipment, and project experience to successfully execute the specific scope of work on the proposed project.

### 3.1 Project Experience and Scale

**Largest Completed Project:** The most fundamental capacity metric. Industry best practice holds that a subcontractor should not be awarded a contract that exceeds **1.5 to 2 times the value of their largest successfully completed project** [4]. Awarding a $5 million electrical contract to a firm whose largest prior project was $1.5 million is a high-risk decision regardless of financial health.

**Project Type and Complexity:** Experience must be relevant. A subcontractor with an excellent track record in light commercial work may be completely unqualified for a healthcare or data center project with specialized mechanical, electrical, and plumbing (MEP) requirements. The GC must evaluate the subcontractor's portfolio for:
- Project type (commercial, industrial, healthcare, education, hospitality, data center, etc.)
- Delivery method (Design-Bid-Build, Design-Build, CM at Risk, Integrated Project Delivery)
- Project complexity (occupied renovations, phased construction, LEED/sustainability requirements)
- Contract structure (lump sum, GMP, unit price, T&M)

**Annual Revenue Trend:** A consistent upward revenue trend indicates a growing, healthy firm. Sudden revenue spikes (e.g., doubling in one year) may indicate the firm has taken on more work than its management infrastructure can handle. Sudden revenue declines may indicate loss of key clients or market position.

**Self-Performance Percentage:** The percentage of work the subcontractor self-performs versus sub-subcontracts is a critical capacity indicator. A firm that sub-subcontracts more than 50% of its own scope has limited direct control over quality and schedule.

### 3.2 Workforce and Manpower Assessment

The skilled labor shortage is one of the most significant risk factors in modern construction. A subcontractor's workforce is its most critical operational asset.

**Total Headcount and Composition:**
- Number of direct field employees (W-2 craft workers)
- Number of supervisory/foreman-level employees
- Number of project management and administrative staff
- Ratio of field to office staff

**Labor Sourcing Strategy:** The GC must understand how the subcontractor intends to staff the project. The key distinction is between:
- *Self-performed labor:* W-2 employees on the subcontractor's payroll. This is the most reliable model for quality and schedule control.
- *Brokered labor:* Workers sourced from staffing agencies or labor brokers. This introduces quality variability, potential workers' compensation exposure, and schedule uncertainty [5].
- *Sub-subcontracting:* Further delegation of scope to lower-tier contractors. This adds layers of risk and reduces the GC's visibility into quality and compliance.

**Union Affiliations:** Verification of active collective bargaining agreements (CBAs) and the status of benefit fund contributions. Delinquent union benefit payments are a leading indicator of financial distress and can result in work stoppages.

**Key Personnel Qualifications:**
- Resume and project history of the proposed Project Manager
- Resume and project history of the proposed Superintendent/Foreman
- OSHA 30-hour certifications for supervisory personnel
- Trade certifications and licenses for specialized work (e.g., NFPA 70E for electrical, EPA 608 for HVAC)

### 3.3 Equipment and Technology Capabilities

**Equipment Schedule:** A list of owned equipment, including age, condition, and estimated value. For heavy civil and mechanical subcontractors, equipment is a critical capacity indicator. The GC should assess:
- Whether the subcontractor owns the equipment necessary for the project scope
- The age and maintenance status of critical equipment
- The subcontractor's plan for equipment that must be rented or leased

**Technology and Software:** Use of project management software (Procore, Viewpoint, Sage), BIM/VDC capabilities, and digital documentation systems indicates a sophisticated, professionally managed firm.

### 3.4 Quality Management System

**Quality Management Plan (QMP):** A formal, written QMP is a strong indicator of a professionally managed firm. The GC should evaluate:
- Whether the subcontractor has a documented QA/QC program
- The qualifications of the designated Quality Control Manager
- The subcontractor's process for Inspection and Test Plans (ITPs)
- History of warranty claims, punch list items, and rework on prior projects

**References from Prior GCs:** Direct calls to prior GC project managers and superintendents are among the most valuable sources of qualitative data. Questions should address:
- Was the subcontractor responsive and collaborative?
- Did they meet the schedule?
- Were there significant punch list or warranty issues?
- Would you hire them again?

## Part IV: Character — Safety, Compliance, and Reputation

Character assessment evaluates the subcontractor's historical behavior, ethical standing, safety culture, legal compliance, and market reputation. This dimension is the most qualitative but is critically important for managing liability and insurance costs.

### 4.1 Safety Performance Metrics

Safety performance is a direct indicator of management quality and operational discipline. A firm that manages safety well generally manages all aspects of its business well. Safety data also has direct financial implications for the GC's own insurance costs.

**Experience Modification Rate (EMR):**
The EMR is a multiplier calculated annually by the NCCI (National Council on Compensation Insurance) or state rating bureaus based on the subcontractor's workers' compensation claims history relative to industry expectations [6]. It is the single most widely used safety metric in prequalification.

| EMR Value | Interpretation | Typical Prequalification Status |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| < 0.75 | Significantly below average claims — excellent safety record | Preferred subcontractor |
| 0.75 – 1.00 | Below average to average claims — acceptable | Standard approval |
| 1.00 | Industry average | Conditional approval, may require safety plan |
| 1.01 – 1.25 | Above average claims — elevated risk | Requires written safety improvement plan |
| > 1.25 | Significantly above average — high risk | Automatic disqualification on most programs |

Many GCs and SDI carriers establish a hard EMR threshold of **1.0 or below** as a condition of prequalification. The EMR must be verified directly from the subcontractor's workers' compensation carrier, not self-reported [6].

**Total Recordable Incident Rate (TRIR):**
TRIR measures the frequency of OSHA-recordable injuries and illnesses per 100 full-time equivalent workers [7].

*Formula:* `TRIR = (Number of Recordable Incidents × 200,000) / Total Hours Worked`

The BLS national average TRIR for specialty trade contractors is approximately 3.0–3.5. GCs typically establish thresholds of **2.0 or below** for prequalification. An TRIR above 4.0 is generally disqualifying.

**Days Away, Restricted, or Transferred (DART) Rate:**
DART measures the severity of incidents, capturing only those that resulted in lost workdays, restricted duty, or job transfer [7].

*Formula:* `DART = (Number of DART Cases × 200,000) / Total Hours Worked`

DART is a more meaningful metric than TRIR because it captures the severity of injuries, not just their frequency. A firm with a high TRIR but low DART may have many minor incidents; a firm with a low TRIR but high DART has fewer but more serious injuries.

**OSHA 300 and 300A Logs:**
The GC should collect OSHA 300 logs for the past 3 to 5 years. These logs provide the underlying data for TRIR and DART calculations and allow the GC to identify patterns in incident types, locations, and causes [8]. The 300A Summary (posted annually) must be signed by a company executive.

**Safety Program Documentation:**
- Written Safety and Health Program (OSHA 1926 compliant)
- Site-Specific Safety Plan (SSSP) capability
- Hazard Communication Program (HazCom/GHS)
- Fall Protection Program
- Confined Space Entry Program (if applicable)
- Lockout/Tagout (LOTO) Program (if applicable)
- Drug and Alcohol Testing Policy
- Safety training records and certifications (OSHA 10/30, First Aid/CPR)
- Designated Safety Officer or Safety Director qualifications

### 4.2 Insurance Coverage Verification

Insurance verification is a critical risk transfer mechanism. The GC must verify not only that the subcontractor has the required coverages but that the policies are properly structured to protect the GC.

**Required Insurance Coverages and Typical Minimum Limits:**

| Coverage Type | Typical Minimum Limits | Key Endorsements Required |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Commercial General Liability (CGL)** | $1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate | CG 20 10 (ongoing ops) + CG 20 37 (completed ops) Additional Insured endorsements; Primary and Non-Contributory; Waiver of Subrogation |
| **Workers' Compensation** | Statutory limits per state | Waiver of Subrogation in favor of GC |
| **Employers' Liability** | $1M / $1M / $1M | — |
| **Commercial Auto Liability** | $1M combined single limit | Additional Insured endorsement |
| **Excess / Umbrella Liability** | $5M–$25M (project-dependent) | Follow-form over all underlying policies |
| **Professional Liability (E&O)** | $1M–$5M (design-build or design-assist scopes) | — |
| **Pollution Liability** | $1M–$5M (environmental scopes) | — |

**Certificate of Insurance (COI) Review:**
The COI must be reviewed for:
- Policy effective and expiration dates (no gaps in coverage)
- Carrier A.M. Best rating (minimum A- / VII)
- Correct additional insured language
- Primary and non-contributory language
- Waiver of subrogation endorsements
- Adequate limits for the proposed contract value

**Surety Bondability Letter:**
A letter from a Treasury-listed surety company (T-List), dated within the last 90 days, confirming the subcontractor's single project and aggregate bonding capacity [4]. This letter is a powerful third-party validation because the surety has independently reviewed the subcontractor's financial statements, WIP schedule, and management team. If the surety is unwilling to provide a bondability letter for a project of the proposed size, the GC should treat this as a significant red flag.

### 4.3 Legal, Regulatory, and Reputational History

**Litigation and Dispute History:**
The AIA A305 *Contractor's Qualification Statement* is the industry-standard form for collecting this data [9]. Key disclosures include:
- Active lawsuits, arbitrations, or mediations with owners, GCs, or suppliers
- History of contract defaults or terminations for cause
- History of liquidated damages assessed against the firm
- Bankruptcy filings (current or within the past 7 years)
- Judgments filed against the firm

**Regulatory Compliance:**
- Active and valid trade licenses in the project jurisdiction (electrical, plumbing, HVAC, etc.)
- Corporate good standing certificate from the Secretary of State
- History of OSHA citations and penalties
- History of labor law violations (prevailing wage, Davis-Bacon, FLSA)
- Environmental violations or EPA citations
- Contractor license suspensions or revocations

**Payment Practices:**
A subcontractor's payment practices toward its own suppliers and sub-subcontractors are a critical character indicator. The GC should investigate:
- Supplier and sub-subcontractor references
- Mechanic's lien filings against the subcontractor's prior projects (public record search)
- UCC filings (Uniform Commercial Code) indicating secured creditors
- Dun & Bradstreet or Experian Business Credit Report

**Banking Relationships:**
A letter from the subcontractor's primary bank confirming the existence of a line of credit, the available balance, and the bank's general assessment of the relationship. A bank that is unwilling to provide a positive reference letter is a significant warning sign.

## Part V: Advanced Risk Scoring and SDI Integration

For GCs managing large portfolios of subcontractors, particularly those utilizing Subcontractor Default Insurance (SDI), the individual data points described above are aggregated into a formal risk scoring system.

### 5.1 Marsh SubSecure and SDI Risk Scoring

Marsh's SubSecure platform, which is approved by every carrier in the SDI marketplace, scores subcontractors across **10 key financial areas** by comparing their financial statements, WIP schedules, income statements, and cash flows to ratios commonly used by sureties in their underwriting reviews [10]. The output is a risk score in four quartiles, representing the subcontractor's probability of default.

The 10 areas scored include:
1. Liquidity (Current Ratio, Quick Ratio, Days of Cash)
2. Working Capital adequacy
3. Underbillings exposure
4. Overbillings management
5. Profitability trends (Gross Margin, Net Margin)
6. Leverage (Debt to Equity, Times Interest Earned)
7. Backlog relative to capacity
8. Accounts Receivable quality and aging
9. Cash flow from operations
10. Overall balance sheet strength and equity trend

### 5.2 Cost Containment Through Prequalification

Rigorous prequalification is not merely a risk management exercise — it is a direct cost containment strategy. For GCs operating under high-deductible insurance programs or group captive structures, every subcontractor-caused incident or default that penetrates the deductible layer represents a direct financial loss to the GC.

By enforcing strict EMR thresholds, financial ratio minimums, and operational standards, the GC:
- Reduces the frequency of workers' compensation claims that pierce the deductible
- Reduces the frequency of general liability claims arising from subcontractor negligence
- Reduces the probability of subcontractor default, which triggers SDI deductibles
- Improves the GC's own EMR over time, reducing workers' compensation premiums
- Strengthens the GC's own surety relationship by demonstrating disciplined risk management

## Part VI: Complete Data Point Checklist

The following is a comprehensive checklist of every data point that should be collected and evaluated during subcontractor prequalification:

### Section A: Company Identity and Basic Information
- Legal entity name and all DBA names
- Federal Employer Identification Number (FEIN) / IRS Form W-9
- State of incorporation and corporate good standing certificate
- Principal office address and all branch locations
- Year founded and years in business under current ownership
- Legal form of entity (S-Corp, C-Corp, LLC, Partnership, Sole Proprietor)
- Ownership structure (names and percentages of all owners with >5% interest)
- Key executive contacts (President, CFO, Project Executive)
- NAICS code(s) for primary work performed
- Trade classifications and primary scope of work

### Section B: Licensing and Regulatory Compliance
- All active trade licenses (number, issuing state, expiration date, certifying agency)
- Contractor license classification and monetary limits
- Specialty certifications (NFPA, EPA, ASME, etc.)
- Union affiliations and CBA status
- Prevailing wage compliance history
- OSHA violation history (citations, penalties, abatement status)
- Labor law violation history (FLSA, Davis-Bacon, state wage laws)
- Environmental compliance history

### Section C: Financial Data Points
- Audited or CPA-reviewed financial statements (last 2–3 fiscal years)
- Most recent interim financial statements (within 90 days)
- Work in Progress (WIP) Schedule (current)
- Accounts Receivable aging schedule
- Accounts Payable aging schedule
- Bank line of credit confirmation letter
- Bonding capacity letter (single and aggregate limits)
- Business credit report (D&B, Experian, or Equifax Business)
- UCC lien search results
- Mechanic's lien history on prior projects

**Financial Ratios to Calculate:**
- Current Ratio (benchmark: ≥ 1.4)
- Quick Ratio (benchmark: ≥ 1.2)
- Days of Cash on Hand (benchmark: ≥ 15 days)
- Working Capital amount (absolute value)
- Underbillings to Working Capital (benchmark: < 15%)
- Gross Profit Margin (benchmark: ≥ 10%)
- Net Profit Margin (benchmark: ≥ 3%)
- Return on Assets (benchmark: ≥ 8%)
- Debt to Equity Ratio (benchmark: ≤ 2.5x)
- Times Interest Earned (benchmark: ≥ 5x)
- Days in Accounts Receivable (benchmark: ≤ 65 days)
- Months in Backlog (benchmark: 3–15 months)
- Backlog to Working Capital (benchmark: ≤ 8x)
- WIP Profit Fade analysis
- Cash to Overbillings ratio

### Section D: Safety Data Points
- EMR for the past 3 years (verified from WC carrier)
- OSHA 300 Logs for the past 3–5 years
- OSHA 300A Summaries for the past 3–5 years
- TRIR calculation (benchmark: ≤ 2.0)
- DART Rate calculation (benchmark: ≤ 1.5)
- Number of OSHA citations in the past 5 years
- Fatality history
- Written Safety and Health Program
- Site-Specific Safety Plan capability
- Drug and Alcohol Testing Policy
- Safety training records and certifications
- Designated Safety Officer qualifications

### Section E: Insurance Data Points
- Certificate of Insurance (COI) with all required endorsements
- CGL policy limits and carrier rating (minimum A- / VII)
- Workers' Compensation policy and carrier rating
- Employers' Liability limits
- Commercial Auto Liability limits
- Excess/Umbrella limits
- Professional Liability (if design-assist scope)
- Pollution Liability (if environmental scope)
- Additional Insured endorsements (CG 20 10 and CG 20 37)
- Primary and Non-Contributory endorsement
- Waiver of Subrogation endorsements
- Confirmation of no coverage gaps or lapses

### Section F: Capacity and Operational Data Points
- Largest successfully completed project (value and description)
- List of 5–10 most relevant completed projects (with GC references)
- Annual revenue for the past 3 years
- Current backlog (value and description of active projects)
- Total headcount (field and office)
- Labor sourcing strategy (self-perform vs. brokered)
- Key personnel resumes (PM, Superintendent)
- Equipment schedule (owned and leased)
- Sub-subcontractor usage and management process
- Quality Management Plan (QMP)
- Technology and software capabilities

### Section G: Character and Reputation Data Points
- Litigation disclosure (active and past 5 years)
- Default and termination history
- Bankruptcy history (past 7 years)
- Judgment history
- Reference checks (minimum 3 GC references, called directly)
- Supplier and sub-subcontractor references
- Banking relationship reference
- Payment practices reputation
- Surety relationship history and bondability status

## Conclusion

Subcontractor prequalification is not a bureaucratic checkbox exercise — it is the foundational risk management practice that determines whether a construction project will be delivered on time, on budget, and without catastrophic financial consequences. The data points outlined in this analysis, drawn from CFMA benchmarks, surety underwriting standards, AIA documentation frameworks, and industry best practices, represent the complete universe of information a GC needs to make an informed, defensible decision about whether a subcontractor is qualified to work a job.

The most sophisticated GCs treat prequalification as a continuous process, not a one-time event. Financial data should be refreshed annually at minimum, and more frequently for subcontractors on large or complex projects. Safety metrics should be monitored in real time. Operational assessments should be updated as the subcontractor's backlog, workforce, and management team evolve.

When integrated with advanced risk management tools — including SDI programs, high-deductible structures, and group captive insurance models — a rigorous prequalification program becomes a powerful cost containment engine that protects the GC's balance sheet, preserves project profitability, and builds a portfolio of trusted, high-performing specialty trade partners.
