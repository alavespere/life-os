# Subcontractor Prequalification Benchmarks by Trade Vertical
## A Deep-Analysis Reference for General Contractors, Risk Managers, and Surety Professionals

**Prepared by:** BuildSafe IQ Research Division  
**Data Sources:** CFMA 2024 Construction Financial Benchmarker, BLS Table 1 (2024 Incidence Rates), ABC STEP NAICS Codes 2025, NCCI Workers Compensation Class Codes, NASBP Surety Underwriting Standards, Marsh SubSecure Framework, IRMI Expert Commentary, ENR Top 400, Gallagher Construction Market Update Winter 2024/2025  
**Reference Date:** April 2026

---

## Executive Summary

The answer to the question "does the trade/vertical of a subcontractor change the rates and benchmarks used to evaluate them?" is an unequivocal **yes** — and the variance is dramatic, not marginal. A roofing contractor and an electrical contractor working on the same commercial project operate in entirely different risk universes. Their workers' compensation base rates differ by a factor of 4–5x. Their OSHA TRIR benchmarks differ by 40–60%. Their general liability underwriting scrutiny differs in kind, not just degree. Their surety underwriting focuses on completely different operational risks. And their financial ratio benchmarks — particularly around working capital intensity, equipment leverage, and backlog concentration — differ based on the capital structure inherent to each trade.

This document provides a complete, trade-by-trade breakdown of every prequalification data point that should be calibrated differently depending on the specialty. It is organized first by the universal prequalification framework, then by each major trade vertical, and finally by a master cross-reference table for rapid comparison.

---

## Part I: Why Trade Vertical Changes Everything

### 1.1 The Core Principle: Risk Is Not Uniform Across Trades

General contractors who apply a single, uniform prequalification scorecard to all subcontractors — regardless of trade — are making a systematic underwriting error. The CFMA, NASBP, AGC, and every major surety company explicitly recognize that the **risk profile of a subcontractor is inseparable from the nature of their work**. The following dimensions all vary materially by trade:

| Dimension | Why It Varies by Trade |
|---|---|
| **Workers' Compensation Rate** | Driven by frequency and severity of injury by occupation type |
| **General Liability Rate** | Driven by completed operations exposure, property damage potential, and fire/water risk |
| **TRIR / DART Benchmark** | BLS publishes separate NAICS-level rates; ABC STEP sets thresholds by NAICS |
| **EMR Baseline** | The "1.0" baseline is calculated against trade peers, not all construction |
| **Bonding Capacity Formula** | Surety underwriters apply trade-specific multipliers to working capital |
| **Financial Ratio Benchmarks** | Capital intensity, receivables cycles, and margin profiles differ by trade |
| **Licensing & Certification Requirements** | Vary dramatically by trade and jurisdiction |
| **Insurance Endorsements Required** | Certain trades require pollution, professional, or product liability endorsements |
| **Largest Single Project Rule** | The 1.5–2x rule is calibrated against trade-typical project sizes |

### 1.2 The NAICS Code as the Foundation

Every data point in this document is anchored to a **NAICS code** — the North American Industry Classification System code that defines the trade. The BLS publishes injury rates by NAICS. The ABC STEP program publishes maximum allowable incidence rates by NAICS. NCCI assigns workers' compensation class codes that map to NAICS. When a GC prequalifies a subcontractor, the first question is: **what is their primary NAICS code?** That single answer determines which benchmarks apply.

## Part II: The Universal Prequalification Framework (Applied by Trade)

Before diving into trade-specific data, it is important to understand that the same **seven evaluation domains** apply to every subcontractor — but the thresholds, benchmarks, and red flags within each domain are calibrated to the trade.

| Domain | What Changes by Trade |
|---|---|
| **1. Financial Health** | Margin benchmarks, current ratio norms, WIP intensity |
| **2. Safety Performance** | TRIR, DART, EMR thresholds tied to NAICS-specific BLS rates |
| **3. Insurance Coverage** | Required limits, endorsements, and specialty coverages |
| **4. Bonding & Surety** | Capacity formulas, single/aggregate limits, trade-specific underwriting |
| **5. Licensing & Compliance** | State licensing requirements vary dramatically by trade |
| **6. Operational Capacity** | Self-perform ratios, equipment schedules, workforce certifications |
| **7. Past Performance & References** | Project type match, complexity match, geographic match |

### Trade 1: Electrical Contractors (NAICS 238210 / SIC 1731)

#### Safety Benchmarks (BLS 2024 / ABC STEP 2025)

Electrical contractors are among the **safer specialty trades** in terms of recordable injury frequency, but they carry unique **severity and fatality risk** due to electrocution, arc flash, and fall hazards.

| Safety Metric | Electrical (238210) | All Specialty Trades (238) | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **TRIR (Total Recordable Incident Rate)** | **1.8** | 2.3 | 22% below trade average — a positive indicator |
| **DART Rate** | **1.1** | 1.4 | Below average — favorable |
| **Days Away from Work Rate** | **0.7** | 1.0 | Below average |
| **Days Job Transfer/Restriction** | **0.3** | 0.5 | Below average |
| **Other Recordable Cases** | **0.8** | 0.9 | Near average |
| **ABC STEP Silver Max TRIR** | **3.0** | — | Maximum allowable for Silver certification |
| **ABC STEP Gold Max (BLS Avg)** | **2.0** | — | Benchmark for Gold certification |
| **ABC STEP Platinum Max** | **1.5** | — | High-performance threshold |
| **ABC STEP Diamond Max** | **1.0** | — | Elite safety performance |

**Fatality Context:** Despite lower TRIR, electricians have a fatality rate of **2.89 per 100,000 workers** (ESFI data). Electrocution is one of the "Fatal Four" in construction. A low TRIR with any electrocution fatality is a significant red flag — it suggests the sub may be underreporting non-fatal incidents.

**EMR Benchmarks (2025):**
- Industry average EMR range: **0.82–0.93**
- Top quartile: **0.60–0.75**
- Acceptable threshold for most GC prequalification: **≤ 1.0**
- Red flag threshold: **> 1.15**
- Disqualifying threshold (most programs): **> 1.25**

**Primary Hazards Driving Claims:** Electrical shock and electrocution, arc flash burns, falls from ladders and scaffolding, lockout/tagout (LOTO) failures, and conduit installation strain injuries.

#### Workers' Compensation Profile

| WC Metric | Data |
|---|---|
| **Primary NCCI Class Code** | **5190** — Electrical Work Within Building |
| **Secondary Class Code** | **7538** — Electrical Light/Power Line Construction |
| **5190 Loss Cost Rate (per $100 payroll)** | **~$5.49** (one of the lower construction rates) |
| **7538 Loss Cost Rate (per $100 payroll)** | **~$13.51** (nearly 2.5x higher for line work) |
| **Key Distinction** | Inside building work (5190) vs. outside power line work (7538) — GCs must verify which code applies |
| **Average WC Claim Cost** | $48,500 per claim (construction average; electrical claims trend higher due to severity) |

**Critical Prequalification Point:** An electrical sub that does both inside wiring and outside power line work must be coded correctly. Misclassification of 7538 work as 5190 is a common fraud vector and a major underwriting concern. Verify the split of payroll between codes.

#### General Liability Profile

Electrical contractors sit in a **moderate-to-high GL risk** category. The primary GL exposures are:

- **Fire and property damage** from faulty wiring, panel connections, or improper grounding
- **Completed operations** — electrical failures that manifest months or years after project completion
- **Arc flash** causing property damage to adjacent equipment
- **Water intrusion** from conduit penetrations improperly sealed

**Typical GL Rates (per $1,000 of payroll or revenue):**
- Small electrical contractor ($500K–$2M revenue): **$85–$150/month** for $1M/$2M policy
- Mid-size ($2M–$10M): **$150–$400/month**
- Large ($10M+): Negotiated, often $500–$1,500/month or more

**Required Endorsements for GC Prequalification:**
- CG 20 10 (Additional Insured — Ongoing Operations)
- CG 20 37 (Additional Insured — Completed Operations, 3-year minimum)
- Primary and Non-Contributory endorsement
- Waiver of Subrogation
- **Arc Flash / Electrical Hazard endorsement** (increasingly required)

**Specialty Coverage Considerations:**
- If the sub performs **design-build electrical work**, Professional Liability (E&O) is required
- If the sub installs **solar or EV charging systems**, specialized endorsements may be needed
- If the sub works on **hazardous locations** (Class I, II, III), pollution liability may be required

#### Surety / Bonding Profile

Electrical contractors are **generally bondable** with standard surety programs because their work is well-defined, licensed, and measurable. However, surety underwriters look at several electrical-specific factors:

**Key Surety Underwriting Factors for Electrical Subs:**
1. **License status** — Electrical licensing is one of the most strictly regulated in construction. A lapsed or suspended license is an immediate disqualifier.
2. **Backlog concentration** — Electrical subs that have more than 40% of backlog with a single GC or owner face concentration risk.
3. **Material procurement exposure** — Electrical work is highly material-intensive (copper wire, conduit, switchgear). Surety underwriters assess whether the sub has adequate working capital to fund material purchases before billing.
4. **Change order management** — Electrical scope changes are common. Subs with poor change order documentation have higher default risk.

**Bonding Capacity Rules of Thumb:**
- Single project limit: **8–12x working capital** (slightly below average due to material intensity)
- Aggregate program: **12–18x working capital**
- Minimum working capital for $1M bond: **~$80,000–$125,000**

#### Financial Benchmarks (CFMA / Industry)

| Financial Metric | Electrical Sub Benchmark | All Specialty Trades | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Gross Profit Margin** | **28–35%** | 20–28% | MEP trades command premium margins |
| **Net Profit Margin** | **4–8%** | 3–6% | Higher than GC average |
| **Current Ratio** | **1.4–1.8** | 1.5–2.0 | Slightly lower due to material float |
| **Quick Ratio** | **1.0–1.4** | 1.0–1.5 | |
| **Debt-to-Equity Ratio** | **< 2.0** | < 2.5 | |
| **Days in Accounts Receivable** | **45–60 days** | 45–65 days | |
| **Overhead Rate** | **25–32%** | 25–38% | |
| **Revenue per Employee** | **$250,000–$325,000** | $175,000–$325,000 | Skilled trade premium |
| **Backlog-to-Revenue Ratio** | **0.8–1.5x** | 0.8–1.5x | |

**Key Red Flags Specific to Electrical Subs:**
- Underbillings exceeding 15% of backlog (material-heavy work should be front-loaded)
- Accounts payable to material suppliers past 60 days (copper/conduit suppliers cut credit quickly)
- Rapid revenue growth (>30% YoY) without corresponding increase in licensed journeyman headcount
- License held by a single individual who is not an equity owner (key-person risk)

#### Licensing Requirements

Electrical contractors are among the **most heavily licensed** trades in the U.S. Prequalification must verify:

- **State Electrical Contractor License** (required in all 50 states, though requirements vary)
- **Master Electrician License** held by the company's qualifying individual
- **Journeyman Electrician Licenses** for field personnel
- **Low Voltage / Fire Alarm License** if performing those scopes (separate license in most states)
- **NICET Certification** for fire alarm work
- **NABCEP Certification** for solar PV work (if applicable)

### Trade 2: Plumbing, Heating & Air Conditioning Contractors — HVAC/Plumbing (NAICS 238220 / SIC 1711)

This NAICS code covers the broadest range of MEP work and is notable for having **one of the highest TRIR rates among building equipment contractors** — a counterintuitive finding given that plumbing and HVAC are often perceived as lower-risk trades.

#### Safety Benchmarks (BLS 2024 / ABC STEP 2025)

| Safety Metric | HVAC/Plumbing (238220) | All Specialty Trades (238) | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **TRIR** | **3.0** | 2.3 | **30% above** trade average — significant |
| **DART Rate** | **1.7** | 1.4 | Above average |
| **Days Away from Work** | **1.1** | 1.0 | Slightly above |
| **Days Transfer/Restriction** | **0.6** | 0.5 | Slightly above |
| **Other Recordable Cases** | **1.4** | 0.9 | **55% above average** — indicates high non-lost-time incidents |
| **ABC STEP Silver Max** | **4.35** | — | |
| **ABC STEP Gold Max (BLS Avg)** | **2.9** | — | |
| **ABC STEP Platinum Max** | **2.175** | — | |
| **ABC STEP Diamond Max** | **1.45** | — | |

**Why HVAC/Plumbing Has a High TRIR:** The combination of confined space entry (mechanical rooms, crawl spaces), overhead work, chemical exposure (refrigerants, flux, solvents), burn hazards (soldering, torch work), and ergonomic strain from awkward positions in tight spaces drives a high frequency of recordable incidents — even though fatalities are less common than in roofing or steel work.

**EMR Benchmarks (2025):**
- Industry average EMR range: **0.92–1.03**
- Top quartile: **0.70–0.85**
- Acceptable threshold: **≤ 1.0**
- Red flag: **> 1.15**

**Sub-Trade Distinction:** HVAC and plumbing are separate disciplines within this NAICS code. A mechanical contractor doing large commercial HVAC systems has a very different risk profile than a residential plumber. GCs should request **payroll split by sub-classification** and verify that the EMR reflects the actual work being performed.

#### Workers' Compensation Profile

| WC Metric | Data |
|---|---|
| **Primary NCCI Class Code (Plumbing)** | **5183** — Plumbing |
| **Primary NCCI Class Code (HVAC)** | **5536** — Air Conditioning |
| **5183 Loss Cost Rate** | **~$7.57 per $100 payroll** |
| **5536 Loss Cost Rate** | **~$7.41 per $100 payroll** |
| **Refrigerant Handling** | May trigger additional class codes for chemical exposure |
| **Sheet Metal Ductwork** | May require separate sheet metal class code |

**Critical Prequalification Point:** HVAC contractors that fabricate their own ductwork in a shop have a manufacturing exposure that requires a separate workers' comp class code. Verify whether the sub has a fabrication shop and whether it is properly coded.

#### General Liability Profile

HVAC and plumbing contractors are in the **highest GL risk cluster** identified by major carriers. The primary reason: **water damage is the most common and expensive GL claim in construction**, and both trades introduce or redirect water.

**Primary GL Exposures:**
- Water damage from pipe failures, improper connections, or pressure testing errors
- Refrigerant leaks causing property damage or environmental liability
- Carbon monoxide incidents from improper gas line or furnace installation
- Torch/hot work fire exposure (soldering copper pipe)
- Completed operations — failures that manifest long after project completion

**Typical GL Rates:**
- Small HVAC/plumbing sub ($500K–$2M): **$75–$140/month**
- Mid-size ($2M–$10M): **$200–$500/month**
- Large ($10M+): Negotiated

**Required Endorsements:**
- CG 20 10 and CG 20 37 (standard)
- Primary/Non-Contributory
- Waiver of Subrogation
- **Contractors Pollution Liability (CPL)** — Required if handling refrigerants, chemicals, or working near environmental hazards
- **Professional Liability** — Required if performing mechanical engineering design

**Special Note on Fire Sprinkler Sub-Specialty:** If the HVAC/plumbing sub also installs fire suppression systems, this is treated as a **separate, higher-risk class** by underwriters. Fire sprinkler installation is among the hardest GL classes to place because a small error can affect an entire building's fire protection system and trigger massive water damage. Require separate limits and endorsements for this scope.

#### Financial Benchmarks

| Financial Metric | HVAC/Plumbing Benchmark | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Gross Profit Margin** | **28–35%** | MEP premium |
| **Net Profit Margin** | **4–7%** | |
| **Current Ratio** | **1.4–1.9** | |
| **Days in AR** | **45–65 days** | |
| **Overhead Rate** | **28–35%** | Higher due to service vehicles, tools |
| **Revenue per Employee** | **$200,000–$300,000** | |

### Trade 3: Roofing Contractors (NAICS 238160 / SIC 1761)

Roofing is the **highest-risk specialty trade** from a workers' compensation and general liability perspective. It consistently ranks at or near the top of every risk metric in construction.

#### Safety Benchmarks (BLS 2024 / ABC STEP 2025)

| Safety Metric | Roofing (238160) | All Specialty Trades (238) | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **TRIR** | **2.4** | 2.3 | Near average — but misleading (see below) |
| **DART Rate** | **1.7** | 1.4 | **21% above** average |
| **Days Away from Work** | **1.1** | 1.0 | Above average |
| **Days Transfer/Restriction** | **0.6** | 0.5 | Above average |
| **ABC STEP Silver Max** | **4.8** | — | Highest allowable rate among common trades |
| **ABC STEP Gold Max (BLS Avg)** | **3.2** | — | |
| **ABC STEP Platinum Max** | **2.4** | — | |
| **ABC STEP Diamond Max** | **1.6** | — | |

**Why the TRIR Appears Lower Than Expected:** Roofing has a well-documented **underreporting problem**. Falls from roofs often result in fatalities or severe injuries that are reported, but non-fatal incidents (strains, heat illness, minor lacerations) are frequently not recorded. The fatality rate for roofers is among the highest in all of construction at **2.56 per 100,000 workers** (ESFI). A roofing sub with a TRIR below 1.5 should be scrutinized carefully — it may indicate underreporting rather than genuine safety excellence.

**EMR Benchmarks (2025):**
- Industry average EMR range: **1.08–1.22** (the only major trade where the average EMR exceeds 1.0)
- Top quartile: **0.75–0.95**
- Acceptable threshold: **≤ 1.0** (stricter than other trades)
- Red flag: **> 1.25**
- Disqualifying: **> 1.40**

**Critical Distinction:** Because the roofing industry average EMR exceeds 1.0, a roofing sub with an EMR of 1.0 is actually performing **better than average for their trade**. GCs should not apply the same EMR threshold to roofers as they do to electrical or HVAC subs. The appropriate comparison is always **within-trade**.

#### Workers' Compensation Profile

| WC Metric | Data |
|---|---|
| **Primary NCCI Class Code (Commercial)** | **5545** — Roofing NOC |
| **Felt/Pitch Roofing** | **5547** — Roofing/Comm/Felt & Pitch |
| **Sheet Metal Flat Roofing** | **5538** — Roofing/Comm/Sht Mtl Flat |
| **5545 Loss Cost Rate** | **~$25.15 per $100 payroll** — among the highest in construction |
| **5547 Loss Cost Rate** | **~$23.01 per $100 payroll** |
| **5538 Loss Cost Rate** | **~$9.48 per $100 payroll** (sheet metal flat roofing is lower risk) |
| **Ratio to Electrical (5190)** | Roofing (5545) is **~4.6x more expensive** than electrical (5190) |

**Critical Prequalification Point:** The type of roofing matters enormously. A sub that does primarily **TPO/EPDM membrane roofing** (5538) has a dramatically lower WC cost than one doing **built-up roofing with hot tar** (5547). Verify the specific roofing system types in the sub's scope.

#### General Liability Profile

Roofing is consistently ranked as the **#1 hardest trade to place for general liability** in the U.S. market. The reasons are structural:

- Work is performed on someone else's building, directly above occupied or finished space
- Completed operations exposure is extremely long-tail (roof leaks can appear years later)
- Hot work (torch-applied membranes) creates fire ignition risk
- Water intrusion below the work area can damage expensive interiors, electronics, and inventory
- Hail and wind damage claims often trigger disputes about pre-existing vs. contractor-caused damage

**Typical GL Rates:**
- Small roofing sub ($500K–$2M): **$150–$300/month** (2–3x the electrical rate)
- Mid-size ($2M–$10M): **$400–$1,000/month**
- Large ($10M+): Often placed in the E&S (Excess & Surplus) market

**Required Endorsements:**
- CG 20 10 and CG 20 37 (minimum 5-year completed operations for commercial roofing)
- Primary/Non-Contributory
- Waiver of Subrogation
- **Hot Work endorsement** (for torch-applied systems)
- **Completed Operations aggregate equal to general aggregate** (not shared)
- Many GCs require **$2M/$4M limits** for roofing (vs. $1M/$2M for lower-risk trades)

**E&S Market Reality:** Many roofing contractors — particularly those doing commercial built-up or torch-applied work — cannot obtain GL coverage in the admitted market and must go to the E&S market. This is not automatically disqualifying, but it requires the GC to verify that the E&S carrier is financially rated (A- or better by AM Best) and that the policy form does not contain exclusions that would leave the GC exposed.

#### Financial Benchmarks

| Financial Metric | Roofing Benchmark | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Gross Profit Margin** | **20–30%** | Lower than MEP due to material intensity |
| **Net Profit Margin** | **3–6%** | Thin margins; weather and warranty exposure |
| **Current Ratio** | **1.3–1.8** | |
| **Days in AR** | **50–70 days** | Retainage is significant in roofing |
| **Warranty Reserve** | Should carry **2–5% of revenue** in warranty reserves | Red flag if absent |
| **Overhead Rate** | **30–40%** | High insurance costs inflate overhead |

**Key Red Flags Specific to Roofing Subs:**
- No warranty reserve on balance sheet
- Workers' comp policy with "roofing exclusion" (some carriers exclude certain roofing operations)
- GL policy placed with non-admitted carrier rated below A- by AM Best
- Completed operations coverage limited to 2 years (commercial roofing warranties are typically 10–20 years)
- High concentration of residential re-roofing (storm chasing) work mixed with commercial new construction — these are very different risk profiles

#### General Liability Profile

Concrete contractors have **moderate-to-high GL exposure** driven primarily by:
- Formwork failure causing property damage or injury
- Concrete pump truck operations (third-party property damage)
- Defective concrete work (strength failures, cracking, delamination) — long-tail completed operations
- Chemical burns to workers or third parties from concrete splatter

**Key Red Flag:** Concrete subs with very low equipment on their balance sheet relative to revenue may be renting all equipment — which is fine operationally but indicates lower asset backing for bonding purposes.

### Trade 5: Structural Steel & Precast Concrete (NAICS 238120 / SIC 1791)

Steel erection is the **most dangerous specialty trade in construction** from a fatality and severity perspective.

#### Safety Benchmarks (BLS 2024 / ABC STEP 2025)

| Safety Metric | Steel/Precast (238120) | All Specialty Trades (238) | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **TRIR** | **2.8** | 2.3 | **22% above** average |
| **DART Rate** | **1.3** | 1.4 | Near average (but severity is extreme) |
| **Days Away from Work** | **0.8** | 1.0 | Below average — but fatalities skew this |
| **ABC STEP Silver Max** | **5.4** | — | Second highest allowable rate |
| **ABC STEP Gold Max (BLS Avg)** | **3.6** | — | |
| **ABC STEP Platinum Max** | **2.7** | — | |
| **ABC STEP Diamond Max** | **1.8** | — | |

**The Fatality Problem:** Steel erection has one of the highest **fatality rates** in construction. Falls from structural steel, struck-by incidents from crane operations, and collapse of partially erected structures are all catastrophic events. The TRIR may appear moderate because many steel erection incidents are fatal (and thus not counted as "recordable" in the same way as non-fatal incidents). A GC evaluating a steel erection sub must look beyond TRIR to **fatality history, crane operator certifications, and OSHA 1926 Subpart R compliance**.

**EMR Benchmarks (2025):**
- Industry average EMR range: **0.90–1.05**
- Top quartile: **0.65–0.80**
- Acceptable threshold: **≤ 1.0** (strict — given severity)
- Red flag: **> 1.15**

#### Workers' Compensation Profile

| WC Metric | Data |
|---|---|
| **Primary NCCI Class Code (High-Rise)** | **5069** — Iron/Steel Erection, not over 2 stories |
| **High-Rise Steel** | **5059** — Iron/Steel Frame Structure, 1–2 stories |
| **5069 Loss Cost Rate** | **~$60.61 per $100 payroll** — the HIGHEST rate in construction |
| **5059 Loss Cost Rate** | **~$35.15 per $100 payroll** — also extremely high |
| **Comparison** | Steel erection (5069) is **~11x more expensive** than electrical (5190) |

**This is the single most important data point in this entire document for a GC evaluating a steel erection sub.** The workers' compensation cost alone is a major financial stress point for steel erectors. A sub with inadequate working capital cannot sustain these WC premiums through a long project without cash flow problems.

#### General Liability Profile

Steel erection requires **the highest GL limits** of any specialty trade. The potential for catastrophic property damage (crane collapse, steel drop) and bodily injury (multi-fatality events) means standard $1M/$2M limits are insufficient.

**Minimum GL Limits for Steel Erection:**
- **$2M per occurrence / $4M aggregate** (minimum for most GC programs)
- **$5M–$10M umbrella/excess** required for projects over $10M
- Crane operations may require separate **Riggers Liability** coverage

**Required Endorsements:**
- Standard set plus **Riggers Liability** endorsement
- **Crane Operator Certification** verification (NCCCO certification required)
- **OSHA 1926 Subpart R** compliance documentation

### Trade 6: Framing Contractors (NAICS 238130 / SIC 1751)

Framing is the **highest TRIR trade** in the entire specialty trades sector.

#### Safety Benchmarks (BLS 2024 / ABC STEP 2025)

| Safety Metric | Framing (238130) | All Specialty Trades (238) | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **TRIR** | **4.6** | 2.3 | **100% above** trade average — the highest |

## Part VII: Key Principles for Trade-Calibrated Prequalification

### Principle 1: Never Compare Across Trades Without Normalization

An electrical contractor with a TRIR of 2.0 is performing **below their trade average** (BLS average: 1.8). A roofing contractor with a TRIR of 2.0 is performing **well above their trade average** (BLS average: 2.4). Applying the same threshold to both produces a false result — the electrical sub looks acceptable when it is actually underperforming, and the roofing sub looks marginal when it is actually above average.

### Principle 2: Workers' Comp Class Code Verification Is Non-Negotiable

The difference between NCCI code 5190 (electrical, ~$5.49/100) and 5069 (steel erection, ~$60.61/100) is an **11-fold difference in workers' compensation cost**. A sub that is misclassified — either intentionally or through ignorance — is either paying too little premium (creating an unfunded liability) or too much (indicating poor financial management). Verify the actual class codes on the sub's WC policy declarations page.

### Principle 3: The Insurance Certificate Is Not Enough

A certificate of insurance shows that a policy exists. It does not show:
- Whether the policy has trade-specific exclusions (e.g., "roofing operations excluded")
- Whether the completed operations coverage has a sunset clause
- Whether the carrier is admitted or non-admitted
- Whether the policy is on an occurrence or claims-made basis
- Whether the limits are shared between multiple projects

For high-risk trades (roofing, steel, fire sprinkler), require a **copy of the actual policy declarations page and the relevant endorsements**, not just the certificate.

### Principle 4: Financial Ratios Must Be Interpreted in Trade Context

A current ratio of 1.3 is a red flag for an electrical contractor (whose trade average is 1.4–1.8) but is perfectly normal for a framing contractor (whose thin margins and material intensity produce lower liquidity). Similarly, a gross margin of 22% is excellent for a concrete sub but mediocre for an electrical sub.

### Principle 5: The Surety Bond Is the Ultimate Prequalification Tool

A surety company that has independently underwritten a subcontractor and issued a performance and payment bond has already conducted the most rigorous prequalification process available. If a sub cannot obtain a surety bond, that is the most powerful negative signal in the entire prequalification framework — regardless of trade.
