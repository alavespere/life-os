# Surety Bonds — Construction Context

A surety bond is a three-party agreement: the surety (bonding company) guarantees to the obligee (GC or owner) that the principal (subcontractor) will perform its contractual obligations. If the principal defaults, the surety steps in — completing the work, hiring a replacement contractor, or paying damages.

**The surety bond is the ultimate subcontractor prequalification tool.** A T-List surety that issues a performance bond has already run the most rigorous financial and character analysis available. If a sub cannot get bonded for the proposed project, that signal overrides all positive financials and references.

---

## Bond Types

| Bond | Purpose | When Required |
|---|---|---|
| **Bid Bond** | Guarantees sub will enter contract at bid price | Required by owner before bid opening; typically 5–10% of bid amount |
| **Performance Bond** | Guarantees completion per contract terms | Required for most public work; increasingly common on private >$500K |
| **Payment Bond** | Guarantees payment to suppliers and sub-subs | Protects GC from mechanic's liens; required on federal work (Miller Act) |

---

## Miller Act (Federal Projects)

For federal construction contracts over $150K, the prime contractor must provide both a performance bond (100% of contract value) and a payment bond (100% of contract value). This protects subs and suppliers who have no lien rights on federal property. Most states have "Little Miller Acts" with similar requirements for state/municipal contracts.

## Bonding Capacity by Trade

| Trade | Typical Single Project Limit | Typical Aggregate Limit |
|---|---|---|
| Electrical | 8–12x working capital | 12–18x working capital |
| HVAC/Plumbing | 8–12x working capital | 12–18x working capital |
| Concrete | 8–10x working capital | 12–15x working capital |
| Steel | 7–10x working capital | 10–15x working capital |
| Roofing | 7–10x working capital | 10–15x working capital |
| Framing | 7–10x working capital | 10–15x working capital |

The bonding capacity letter from a T-List surety, dated within 90 days, must confirm both the single-project limit and aggregate limit specific to the proposed project value.

---

## SDI vs. Surety Bonds

Subcontractor Default Insurance (SDI) is an alternative to requiring bonds on every sub. The GC purchases a blanket policy that covers sub defaults. Advantages: faster response than surety, no surety cost markup to GC. Disadvantages: GC retains first-dollar risk (deductible), intensive prequal program required to maintain underwriter approval.

For SDI programs, subs are scored across 10 financial dimensions (Marsh SubSecure / SDI framework) — see [[financial-ratios]] for the scoring model.
