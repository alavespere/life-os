## Chapter 2: Differing Site Conditions

**Summary:** Addresses when contractors can recover additional costs for subsurface or physical conditions that differ from what contract documents indicated (Type I) or from what is normal for that type of work (Type II). One of the highest-frequency claims categories on ground-up construction.

**Key Principles:**
- Contractor must provide timely written notice upon discovery of the condition
- Type I: compare actual conditions to what the contract documents affirmatively represented
- Type II: compare to what an experienced contractor would normally expect
- "Contractor investigated all site conditions" exculpatory language is strictly construed; does not eliminate Type I claims

> **BSIQ Application — Risk Scoring:**
> Differing site conditions are among the top 3 claim categories on below-grade and site work projects. Risk scoring inputs: (1) Quality of geotechnical investigation (Phase I / Phase II / none); (2) % of project that is below grade; (3) Urban infill vs. greenfield; (4) Age of existing underground infrastructure; (5) Presence of known contamination. Projects with no geotech = automatic elevated risk flag. Content hook: "The One Report That Can Save Your Construction Claim."

## Chapter 3: Contractor Licensing

**Summary:** Covers consequences of performing construction work without proper licensure — courts in many states bar unlicensed contractors from enforcing contracts or recovering payment even for properly-performed work. Licensing requirements vary by state, trade, and project type.

**Key Principles:**
- Unlicensed contractor may be barred from suing for payment even if work was defect-free
- Subcontractors' licensing status matters — prime may face liability for sub's unlicensed work
- License suspension or revocation mid-project creates significant liability exposure
- Licensing requirements are strictly construed; courts do not apply equitable exceptions

> **BSIQ Application — Underwriting:**
> Contractor license status is a Tier 1 underwriting check. BSIQ submission intake should capture: license number, state(s) licensed, expiration date, disciplinary action history. A suspended or expired license is an immediate underwriting red flag. This is a fast, automatable API check that BSIQ can build into the submission workflow.

## Chapter 4: Disputes and Claims

**Summary:** Covers the mechanics of presenting and pursuing construction claims — notice requirements, claim documentation, ADR clauses, and the escalation path from informal project-level resolution through formal dispute resolution. Notice requirements are often the most expensive failure point.

**Key Principles:**
- Notice deadlines are strictly enforced — failure is typically an absolute bar (not just affecting damages)
- "Claims made" policy analogy applies: knowing about a potential problem is NOT the same as giving formal notice
- Contemporaneously-generated documentation (daily reports, photos, RFIs, correspondence) carries far more weight than reconstructed records
- Dispute resolution clauses (arbitration, mediation, litigation) are enforceable and affect forum, speed, and cost

> **BSIQ Application — Risk Scoring + Content:**
> Documentation quality is one of the most predictive risk factors across ALL claim types. BSIQ intake questions: Does GC have a formal claims administration process? Are daily reports maintained? Is there an RFI tracking system? Content series: "5 Things Your Daily Reports Must Capture to Protect a Construction Claim."

## Chapter 5: Scope of Work

**Summary:** Addresses what is and isn't included in a contractor's scope, and consequences of scope gaps, overlaps, and ambiguities. Covers the duty to coordinate when contract documents conflict or are incomplete.

**Key Principles:**
- Ambiguities in scope are typically construed against the drafter
- Contractor has a limited duty to flag apparent errors in contract documents before beginning work
- Scope exclusions must be explicitly stated — implied exclusions are rarely recognized
- Course of dealing and prior practice between parties can expand or modify written scope

> **BSIQ Application — Risk Scoring:**
> Incomplete contract documents at bid time are a major risk predictor. Scoring inputs: Are contract documents 100% complete at award? Is there a defined scope exclusion list? Has the contractor performed a pre-bid document review? Design-assist and early procurement strategies often create scope ambiguity — flag these.

## Chapter 6: Design-Build

**Summary:** Unique risk allocation in design-build delivery, where the contractor assumes design liability. Covers the implied warranty of fitness for purpose in D-B work, the role of owner's program requirements, and A/E sub-consultant relationships.

**Key Principles:**
- Design-builder has an implied warranty that the completed project will be fit for its intended purpose
- Owner's program requirements (or "performance specifications") define the D-B obligation — owner takes risk of under-specified programs
- D-B contractor cannot disclaim design liability the way a traditional contractor disclaims the architect's design
- A/E firms acting as D-B sub-consultants should understand their liability runs to the D-B contractor, not the owner

> **BSIQ Application — Content + Risk Scoring:**
> Design-build is the fastest-growing delivery method and the most commonly mis-insured. Risk scoring: D-B contract = mandatory professional liability verification; confirm D-B contractor carries E&O (not just CGL). Content: "Design-Build Is Not Traditional Construction — Here's How Your Insurance Must Change." This is a premium LinkedIn article for the A/E and GC audience.

## Chapter 7: Professional Liability (Design Professional)

**Summary:** Comprehensive treatment of A/E liability — standard of care, common claim triggers (shop drawing review, certifications, coordination failures, site observation), and the limits of exculpatory contract language.

**Key Principles:**
- Standard of care = what a similarly situated design professional in the same locality, with the same knowledge, would have done — NOT perfection
- Design professionals are NOT guarantors of their designs; they warrant professional effort
- Shop drawing review: "approved" stamp creates risk even with disclaimer language; courts look at the practical effect of the review
- Certifications carry warranty exposure — A/E should carefully parse the language of every certification they sign
- Coordination of consultant documents is an enforceable design professional duty

> **BSIQ Application — E&O Underwriting:**
> Critical chapter for BSIQ's E&O placement. Key intake questions: Does the firm use a standard limited shop drawing review stamp? What is their site observation protocol? Do contracts limit liability for certifications? Does the firm have QC procedures? The answers directly map to risk tier. Content: "The 3 Times an Architect Creates Warranty Exposure Without Knowing It."

## Chapter 8: Environmental

**Summary:** Environmental liability in construction — CERCLA, hazardous materials encountered during construction (asbestos, lead, PCBs, mold), and the allocation of environmental cleanup costs between owners and contractors.

**Key Principles:**
- Contractors who disturb pre-existing contamination may become "operators" liable under CERCLA
- Owner indemnification for pre-existing conditions is critical — must be express and in writing
- Mold claims increasingly blur the line between construction defect and environmental claims
- Pollution exclusions in CGL policies are broadly (and aggressively) applied by carriers

> **BSIQ Application — Risk Scoring:**
> Environmental risk factor = prior industrial use, brownfield designation, age of existing building materials (pre-1980 = asbestos/lead flag), or presence of underground storage tanks. BSIQ risk scoring should ask these questions at intake. Elevated CGL submission complexity — carriers will want pollution coverage or exclusion confirmation. This is also a content opportunity: "What Happens When Your Excavation Hits Contamination You Didn't Know Was There."

## Chapter 9: Bidding

**Summary:** Bid preparation and submission, bid mistakes, bid bond obligations, withdrawal rights, bid protests, and government contracting under the FAR and Miller Act. The power of attorney issue on bid bonds has resulted in significant project bid rejections.

**Key Cases:**
- *Kemper Construction Co.* (Corps of Engineers) — Bid rejected because power of attorney accompanying bid bond was a faxed/photocopied document, not an original
- *Scheppler Industries, Inc.* (GSBCA) — Same result; GAO/Board consistently require original power of attorney documents with bid bonds

**Key Principles:**
- Bid mistakes are difficult to withdraw after public bid opening — unilateral mistake doctrine has narrow application in most jurisdictions
- Bid bonds must include original signatures and original, current power of attorney — faxed or photocopied instruments are grounds for rejection as non-responsive
- Miller Act (1935, amended 1999): payment bonds required on federal projects >$100K; 2000 Act raised bond to 100% of contract value; written notice to prime within 90 days of last work by sub; suit within 1 year

> **BSIQ Application — Content:**
> Strong LinkedIn content for federal contracting market: "Bid Bond Basics: The Power of Attorney Mistake That Gets Bids Thrown Out." Also relevant for BSIQ's surety placement practice.

## Chapter 10: Changes

**Summary:** The owner's right to order changes, contractor entitlement to price and time adjustments, the constructive change doctrine (owner actions that constitute changes without formal change orders), and the cardinal change doctrine.

**Key Principles:**
- Changes clause gives owner unilateral right to change within the general scope of the contract
- Constructive change: even without a formal CO, contractor may have a compensable claim if owner's actions/inactions caused work outside original scope
- Cardinal change doctrine: a cumulative pattern of changes that fundamentally alters the character of the project may entitle contractor to treat the contract as terminated
- Changes must be documented contemporaneously — the cost and time impact should be recorded when the work is performed, not reconstructed at claims time

> **BSIQ Application — Risk Scoring:**
> Change order frequency is a leading indicator of project distress and claim risk. Model inputs: cumulative CO value as % of original contract; timing pattern of COs (early = design issues; late = site condition issues); ratio of owner-directed vs. contractor-requested COs. High CO rate + late-stage = elevated claims risk flag.

## Chapter 11: Pay When Paid / Pay If Paid

**Summary:** Critical legal distinction with major financial consequences. "Pay when paid" = timing mechanism (sub must wait, but prime ultimately must pay). "Pay if paid" = condition precedent (sub takes the risk of owner non-payment). Enforceability varies dramatically by state.

**Key Principles:**
- Clear, unambiguous, explicit language is required for courts to find a true "pay if paid" condition precedent
- "Flow-down" clauses incorporating the prime contract by reference may or may not be enforced as pay-if-paid
- California, New York, and several other states prohibit or severely limit pay-if-paid clauses for subcontractors
- Even where enforced, pay-if-paid clauses have exceptions for owner insolvency and GC wrongful withholding

> **BSIQ Application — Content + Underwriting:**
> One of the most misunderstood contractor contract provisions. Strong content: "Pay When Paid vs. Pay If Paid — They Are Not the Same Clause (And One Can Destroy Your Subcontract Cash Flow)." Underwriting input: pay-if-paid contracts increase subcontractor financial exposure — flag in risk scoring. For BSIQ brokerage: contractors with pay-if-paid exposure need stronger bonding and higher working capital reserves.

## Chapter 12: Consequential Damages

**Summary:** When parties can recover consequential damages in construction disputes, and how courts treat mutual waivers of consequential damages (found in standard AIA and EJCDC contracts). Lost profits, lost business opportunities, and extended overhead.

**Key Principles:**
- AIA A201 and B101 / EJCDC contracts contain mutual waivers of consequential damages — these are broadly enforced in commercial construction
- Lost profits and lost business opportunity are typically consequential damages — not recoverable under AIA-form contracts
- Extended overhead during an owner-caused delay is generally DIRECT damages, not consequential — therefore NOT waived by the mutual waiver clause
- Courts strictly enforce consequential damage waivers in commercial (non-consumer) contracts

> **BSIQ Application — Content:**
> Major misunderstanding in the field. Content: "What the Mutual Waiver of Consequential Damages in Your AIA Contract Actually Eliminates — And What It Doesn't." Also relevant for E&O submissions: does the A/E firm's standard contract include a consequential damage waiver?

## Chapter 13: Joint Ventures

**Summary:** Liability allocation among joint venture partners — joint and several liability to third parties, contribution rights among partners, dissolution, and insurance requirements.

**Key Principles:**
- JV partners are jointly and severally liable to third parties regardless of internal liability allocation in the JV agreement
- JV agreement governs contribution among partners internally but does not limit third-party claims
- Insurance in JV name vs. individual partners' policies creates complex coverage questions
- Formal dissolution of a JV requires specific documentation to limit continuing liability exposure

> **BSIQ Application — Underwriting:**
> JV status is a material underwriting fact for both CGL and E&O submissions. BSIQ intake should ask: Is the named insured a joint venture? If yes, identify all partners, ownership percentages, and JV agreement terms. Both individual partner policies AND a JV-specific policy may be needed. Content: "Joint Ventures and Construction Insurance — The Coverage Gap Nobody Talks About."

## Chapter 14: Construction Defects

**Summary:** One of the most litigation-intensive areas. Covers the three defect categories (design, workmanship, material); statutes of limitations and repose; the economic loss rule; and the interaction with insurance coverage.

**Key Principles:**
- Three distinct categories: design defects (A/E liability), workmanship defects (contractor liability), material defects (manufacturer/supplier liability)
- Latent defects: discovery rule extends limitations period — claim accrues when plaintiff discovered or reasonably should have discovered the defect
- Economic loss rule: in many states, purely economic loss without personal injury or physical property damage to third-party property = no tort claim; must sue in contract
- Construction defect mass tort actions (EIFS, polybutylene pipe, Chinese drywall) demonstrate how product-type defects spread across multiple projects and insurers

> **BSIQ Application — Risk Scoring:**
> Key risk scoring inputs: Does project involve high-frequency defect categories? EIFS/stucco (water intrusion), underground plumbing (polybutylene, lead), waterproofing, roofing, structural steel connections, curtain wall. These scopes = elevated risk tier. Content: "The 5 Construction Scopes That Drive the Most Defect Claims — And How to Document Your Way Out of Them."

## Chapter 15: Delay

**Summary:** Comprehensive treatment of delay types — excusable, compensable, inexcusable — and the concurrent delay doctrine. Force majeure, owner-caused delay, contractor-caused delay, and how CPM scheduling separates them.

**Key Principles:**
- Concurrent delay: when both owner and contractor cause delay simultaneously, contractor typically cannot recover delay damages — the concurrent owner-caused delay wipes out the contractor's entitlement for that period
- "No damage for delay" clauses: enforceable in most states with exceptions for active owner interference, gross negligence, fraud, or owner preventing performance
- Float belongs to the project (neither party can "use up" float to avoid paying for true delay)
- Delay damages = extended general conditions + extended home office overhead (Eichleay formula) + material escalation

> **BSIQ Application — Risk Scoring + Content:**
> Delay is among the top 3 causes of construction claims and the most common trigger for project disputes. Risk scoring: Are there milestone/liquidated damages provisions? What is the LD amount vs. project margin? Does the GC have CPM scheduling with regular updates? Content: "When Does a Delay Become Compensable — A Construction Manager's Complete Guide."

## Chapter 16: Insurance
*(Most extensively documented chapter — pages 194–221)*

### Article: Construction Defects and CGL Insurance
**Authors:** John B. Lennes, Jr. (Zurich NA Construction) and J. Kent Holland Jr.

### I. CGL Policy Triggers for Construction Defect Claims
The central battleground: whether defective construction workmanship constitutes an "occurrence" under a CGL policy.
- Most CGL policies define occurrence as "an accident, including continuous or repeated exposure to substantially the same general harmful conditions"
- Courts are split: many hold that defective workmanship is foreseeable and therefore NOT an "occurrence"; others hold that resulting damage to other property IS an occurrence
- **Core principle:** CGL is NOT a performance bond. It does not pay to redo defective work. It pays for damage that defective work causes to OTHER property.

### II. CGL Policy Exclusions — The Eight Key Exclusions for Defect Claims
| # | Exclusion | What It Blocks |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Contractual Liability | Liability assumed under contract (except "insured contracts") |
| 2 | j(5) Faulty Workmanship | Damage to property while contractor is working on it |
| 3 | j(6) Faulty Workmanship | Property that must be restored/repaired because of defective work |
| 4 | Your Work | Damage to "your work" after completion — the subcontractor exception is crucial here |
| 5 | Work Product | Your completed work product |
| 6 | Sistership | Recall of similar products/work |
| 7 | Premises Alienated/Owned | Property you no longer own or still own |
| 8 | Impaired Property | Property that could be made usable by repair/replacement of your work |

> **Subcontractor exception to "Your Work" exclusion:** If the damaged work was performed by a subcontractor on the contractor's behalf, the exclusion does NOT apply. This is why general contractors need to confirm sub insurance and additional insured status.

### III. Key Cases — Chapter 16
| Case | Court/Year | Holding |
|---|---|---|
| *Presley Homes v. American States* | — | Who must defend the construction defect suit? Duty to defend analysis — broader than duty to indemnify |
| *American Country Ins. v. Cline* | — | AI endorsement: additional insured coverage limited to vicarious liability for named insured's work |
| *Prisco Serena Stunn Architects v. Liberty Mutual* | 4th Cir. | No CGL coverage for professional liability claims even when A/E is additional insured |
| *Laquila Construction v. Pinnacle Concrete/Travelers* | — | Applying j(6) exclusion for replacing defective work |
| *Jamestown Builders v. General Star* | — | Insurance recovery forfeited by failure to timely report claim |
| *St. Paul Insurance Co. v. Texas Dept. of Transportation* | TX App. 1999 | Wrongful refusal to defend AI: duty to defend entire suit once ANY allegation falls within coverage; doubt resolved in insured's favor |
| *Escorp, Inc. v. Liberty Mutual Insurance* | 8th Cir. | Defective inspection services caused "property damage" — CGL coverage found; both duty to defend AND indemnify triggered |
| *Rays v. Valley Forge Insurance Co.* | CA App. 1999 | Roofing contractor's re-roofing advice = "professional services"; no CGL coverage; professional liability policy needed |
| *Structural Building Products Corp. v. Business Insurance Agency* | NY App. Div. 2001 | No CGL coverage for economic loss from asbestos-containing product removal; no bodily injury or property damage |
| *American Fire & Casualty Co. v. Broeren Russo Construction* | C.D. Ill. 1999 | EIFS water intrusion: damages were natural consequence of contractor's breach of contract; not an "occurrence" |
| *Homsey Architects, Inc. v. Harry David Zutz Ins.* | Del. Super. 2000 | Claims-made policy; late notice = no coverage; carrier need not prove prejudice; "fundamental to underwriting" |

### IV. Mold
- Mold creates a tripartite coverage battle: Is it general liability (toxic tort), construction defect (CGL), or property damage (property policy)?
- Most CGL policies added mold exclusions after 2002
- Property policies: coverage depends on causation — sudden/accidental water intrusion vs. long-term seepage (typically excluded)

### V. A/E and CM Professional Liability Coverage
- A. **Certifications** — AIA B-141 and EJCDC certification language can create warranty exposure beyond professional judgment
- B. **Warranties/Guarantees** — Architects and engineers must avoid language that could be read as guaranteeing outcomes
- C. **Delay in Performance** — A/E can be liable for damages caused by its own delay in delivering documents or services

## Chapter 17: Limitation of Liability

**Summary:** Examines enforceability of contractual LoL clauses capping A/E or contractor liability at a fixed dollar amount. Generally enforced in commercial construction.

### 17.1 LoL Not Applicable to Claims Arising Out of Contract Addendum
**Case:** *Perini Corp. v. City of New York* (2d Cir. 1999, 1999 WL 304376)
- LoL clause in main Agreement did not apply to delay claims arising from an addendum required by federal grant agency
- Court: the addendum was part of the "contract" but Article 53's reference to "the Agreement" did not cover claims based on addendum obligations
- **Risk Management Note:** Draft LoL clauses to expressly cover ALL contract documents — "this Agreement and all addenda, exhibits, and amendments." Ambiguity is resolved against the party trying to enforce the LoL.

**Key Principles:**
- LoL clauses are generally enforceable in commercial construction (distinguishable from consumer contracts)
- The scope of the LoL (which documents, which claims) must be crystal clear
- LoL amount should be commercially reasonable relative to fee — courts have voided unconscionable limitations

> **BSIQ Application — E&O Submissions:**
> LoL clauses directly affect E&O premium and coverage adequacy. A $50K LoL reduces carrier exposure but may leave the A/E exposed to claims above that limit paid out of pocket. BSIQ intake questionnaire should capture: Does the firm use LoL clauses? What is the typical LoL amount? Is the LoL mutual? Answering these questions improves submission quality and pricing accuracy.

## Chapter 18: Scheduling

### 18.1 The Project Schedule: What Is It Good For?
**Author:** David D. Chitester, P.E.; Chitester Management Systems

- Schedule is FIRST a management tool, second a dispute avoidance tool
- Baseline CPM schedule = benchmark against which all delays, changes, and accelerations are measured
- Properly maintained schedule allows contemporaneous identification and pricing of impacts — prevents "end of job claims"
- Owner uses schedule: to decide on time extensions, authorize acceleration, price change orders, decide on termination
- Contractor uses schedule: to price changes with time impacts, demonstrate concurrent delay analysis, prove acceleration entitlement
- **Key warning:** A stale, unmaintained schedule can be WORSE than no schedule in litigation

### 18.2 The Measured Mile: How to Calculate Lost Productivity Claims
**Author:** David D. Chitester, P.E.

**What it is:** Compares contractor's productivity during an unimpacted period vs. an impacted period. The difference = lost productivity attributable to the specific impact.

**Why it's preferred:** Uses contractor's OWN demonstrated performance rather than industry averages — contractor cannot be penalized for being more efficient than average.

**What it requires:** (1) Detailed, contemporaneous production records; (2) Ability to identify discrete impacted vs. unimpacted periods; (3) Good cost-coded time records showing labor hours by activity

**Example from the book (water treatment plant pipe installation):**
- Unimpacted productivity: (1600+1920+1378+1740) / (10+12+8+10) = **166 feet/day**
- Impacted productivity: (1834+1562+1692+1390) / (14+11+12+10) = **138 feet/day**
- Lost productivity impact: 6478 feet ÷ 166 ft/day = 39 days to complete (without impact); actual took 47 days
- **8 additional days** × crew cost = $37,628 claim

### 18.3 Constructive Acceleration: Does Speeding Up Slow You Down?
**Author:** David D. Chitester, P.E.

**Elements required for a constructive acceleration claim:**
1. Delays are excusable or compensable
2. Contractor requested time extension
3. Owner denied or failed to grant extension
4. Owner constructively accelerated (ordered completion by original date despite delay)
5. Contractor actually accelerated
6. Contractor gave notice it expected to be paid for the acceleration

**Real cost of acceleration (often underestimated):**
- Direct: overtime premium pay, additional equipment
- Impact: loss of productivity from fatigue/extended overtime (can increase total by 250%)
- Trade stacking and crowding
- Dilution of supervision
- Re-sequencing inefficiencies
- Procurement premiums for early deliveries

**Schedule reduction vs. schedule compression:**
- Reduction: shorten schedule without increasing costs (by eliminating inefficiencies)
- Compression: shorten schedule but increase costs (overtime, added crews) — this is what creates acceleration claims

### 18.4 Common Pitfalls of Scheduling Software Programs
**Author:** J. W. Martin III, P.E.; Chitester Management Systems

**The #1 Pitfall — Linked Percent Complete / Remaining Duration:**
- Most scheduling software links % complete to remaining duration by default
- When one is updated, the software auto-calculates the other
- Problem: these should be INDEPENDENT entries — automatically linked values produce artificially "accurate-looking" schedules that are actually wrong
- Costloaded schedules are most vulnerable — % complete must match earned value, not just physical progress

**Example from book:** Electrical activity "Set Motor Control Center, Wire and Terminate" — 10-day duration, $12,000 cost. MCC delivered (10-day item, 90% of cost), but wiring not yet done (remaining work = 9 more days). If statused as 90% complete with 1 day remaining — schedule shows wrong dates for dependent activities AND cost reports will be inaccurate.

**Fix:** Always independently status both % complete AND remaining duration for each activity at each update.

> **BSIQ Application — Risk Scoring:**
> Schedule quality = one of the most predictive indicators of project management sophistication and claim risk. Scoring inputs: Does the project have a baseline CPM schedule? Is it being updated monthly? Does the PM understand the difference between schedule reduction and schedule compression? Does the GC track production rates (Measured Mile capability)? Answering YES to all = lowest scheduling risk tier.
> Content: "The Scheduling Software Setting That Loses Construction Claims" (linked % complete issue — highly shareable among schedulers and PMs).

## Chapter 19: Site Safety Risk and Responsibility

**Key message:** A/E and CM liability for site safety is one of the most jurisdiction-specific areas of construction law. What creates liability in New Jersey may not create it in Pennsylvania. Always seek local counsel.

### 19.2 A/E Knowledge of Danger — Duty to Non-Employees?

| Case | Jurisdiction | Holding |
|---|---|---|
| *Carvalho v. Toll Brothers & Development* | NJ, 1996 | Engineer who had authority to halt work and had actual knowledge of dangerous conditions had duty to exercise reasonable care, regardless of contract language saying otherwise |
| *Herczeg v. Hampton Township Municipal Authority and Bankson Engineers* | PA, 766 A.2d 866 (2001) | PA courts declined to impose liability; A/E's authority to reject work was for benefit of project owner to confirm spec compliance, not for workers' safety; not an "undertaking" to supervise safety |

**The split:** NJ follows the "knowledge + authority to stop = duty" approach. PA and many other states require an affirmative "undertaking" to control safety before imposing liability.

### 19.3 OSHA Construction Standards — Do They Apply to Design Professionals?

| Case | OSHRC Decision | Final Result |
|---|---|---|
| *Secretary of Labor v. Folt-Albert Assoc.* (OSHRC 92-654, 1997) | Engineer NOT subject to OSHA; lacked degree of control over safety | Affirmed — Folt-Albert used standard AIA C727 contract limiting responsibility |
| *Secretary of Labor v. CH2M Hill Central* (OSHRC 89-1712, 1997) | Engineer WAS subject to OSHA; had "global project responsibilities" | **REVERSED** by 7th Circuit: *CH2M Hill, Inc. v. Herman* (7th Cir. 1999) |

**CH2M Hill 7th Circuit ruling (key facts):**
- CH2M Hill drafted contract modifications related to methane gas safety hazard
- BUT MMSD had to approve ALL changes — CH2M Hill could not act independently
- Court: "CH2M Hill did not function as a coordinator of the safety program; the contract specifically removed this responsibility"
- OSHA standards did NOT apply because CH2M Hill "had neither by contract nor by actions engaged in construction work"

### 19.5-19.7 Project Owner and Prime Contractor Safety Liability

| Case | Holding |
|---|---|
| *Kevin Fisher v. Lee & Change* (Tex. App. 2000) | Owner not liable; did not retain control over means/methods; no actual knowledge of dangerous condition |
| *Zamudio v. City and County of San Francisco* (Cal. App. 1999) | Contractual right to inspect for spec compliance ≠ control over site safety; "Liability attaches only when owner retains control over operative details" |
| *Smart v. Chrysler Corp.* (Mo. Court of Appeals) | Chrysler conducted safety meetings but had surrendered job site to GC; no retained control; not liable for sub employee's fall |

**The recurring principle from Ch. 19:** "Liability attaches only when the owner or general contractor retains control over the operative details of the hired work. In the absence of direct management over the means and methods of the independent contractor's work or the provision of the equipment which caused the injury, no legal duty is created."

### 19.8 General Contractor Liable for Sub's Employee Death
**Case:** *Lee Lewis Construction, Inc. v. Harrison* (TX Supreme Court, 2001) — GC responsible for death of KK Glass employee (Jimmy Harrison, fell from 10th floor without lifeline)

**Why the GC was liable:**
- GC had contractually agreed with OWNER to be responsible for safety of ALL people on site
- GC required all subs to adhere to detailed safety manual under penalty of removal
- GC's own superintendent knew and did NOT object to KK Glass employees not using lifeline
- GC had actual, not just contractual, control over fall protection compliance

**Lesson:** If you contractually undertake safety responsibility AND actively exercise it, you own the liability. Subcontract language saying "sub is solely responsible" was outweighed by GC's actual conduct on the site.

### 19.9 Contractor Criminally Indicted
**Case:** *People v. Roger Reagan* (NY, 1999) — GC and president indicted for manslaughter/reckless endangerment; two workers drowned in collapsed trench.
- GC had stopped work, consulted City inspector, cautioned workers about the pipe, and resumed at City's direction
- Grand Jury dismissed charges: defendants did NOT "consciously disregard a substantial risk" — they took steps to avert it
- **Documentation lesson:** The reason the GC survived was that their safety steps were documented and the City inspector interaction was on record

### 19.10 The Seven-Factor Test for A/E Site Safety Liability
Courts use these factors to determine whether a design professional assumed site safety responsibility:
1. Actual supervision and control of the work
2. Retention of the right to supervise and control
3. Constant participation in ongoing activities at the construction site
4. Supervision and coordination of subcontractors
5. Assumption of responsibility for safety practices
6. Authority to issue change orders
7. The right to stop the work

> **BSIQ Application — E&O Underwriting + Risk Scoring:**
> These seven factors ARE the E&O underwriting questionnaire for A/E safety exposure. Any "yes" answer increases OSHA exposure and E&O risk tier. Risk scoring for GC: EMR, written safety manual (yes/no), dedicated safety officer (yes/no), safety training documentation program (yes/no).

## Chapter 20: Statute of Limitations & Other Time Bars

### 20.1 Statute of Repose Bars Claim Filed Within Limitations Period
**Case:** *Two Denver Highlands Ltd. Partnership v. Stanley Structures* (Colo. App. 2000) — Six-year statute of repose barred negligence/products liability suit against design firm; court focused on NATURE OF ACTIVITY (construction services), not the legal label applied by plaintiff

### 20.2 A/E Contract Established Time Limit for Suit
**Case:** *Sarbacker Harbor Court Associates v. Leo A. Daly Co.* (4th Cir. 1999) — AIA B141 contract contained standard limitations clause running from Substantial Completion; court enforced it; Maryland "discovery rule" did not override contractual date in commercial transaction between sophisticated parties

**Risk Management Note:** By establishing a definite cut-off for claims, an A/E can: (1) Limit insurance tail requirements; (2) Reduce E&O premiums; (3) Give carriers a defined exposure window. AIA forms contain this language — it is generally enforced in commercial projects.

### 20.3 Architect Cannot Enforce Time Limit After Misrepresentation
**Case:** *Northridge Homes, Inc. v. John W. French & Associates* (Mass. 1999) — Equitable estoppel prevented A/E from asserting contractual time limitation because defendants had made "knowing or reckless misrepresentations" that leaks were not caused by structural problems

> **BSIQ Application — E&O Underwriting:**
> Statute of repose is one of the most critical E&O underwriting variables. A 6-year repose state has dramatically less long-tail exposure than a 12-year state. BSIQ should build a state-by-state repose reference table for E&O submissions. Key question: What states does the firm practice in? This maps directly to the appropriate retroactive date and extended reporting period requirements.

## Chapter 21: Statute of Repose

### 21.1 Statute of Repose Passes Constitutional Test
Washington State six-year repose upheld (WA Supreme Court), even though court felt result was "distasteful and unfair." Barred condominium association claims against A/E who designed condominiums that slid in heavy rains. Court: the statute is constitutional; its purpose is to protect the construction industry from latent defect claims that arise decades after completion.

### 21.2 Survey of State Statutes of Repose
*(State-by-state survey table in book; repose periods typically range from 4 years [TN] to 15 years [some states have none])*

### 21.3 Statute of Repose Applies to BOTH Contract and Tort Claims
Maryland Court of Appeals: "The issue should be whether, if the injury or damages arises from the defective and unsafe condition of an improvement to real property, that injury or damage occurs more than ten years after 'the date the entire improvement first became available for its intended use,' and not whether the claim is pleaded as one in contract or tort." Both tort AND breach of contract claims barred by ten-year repose.

> State statute of repose is a competitive intelligence advantage for BSIQ brokerage. Knowing the exact repose period for each state where a client practices allows BSIQ to right-size tail coverage and retroactive dates — avoiding both over-insurance (cost to client) and under-insurance (coverage gap).

## Chapter 22: Waiver of Subrogation

### 22.1 Waiver of Subrogation in Renovation Contract
**Case:** *Mu Chapter of the Sigma Pi Fraternity v. Northeast Construction Services* (NY Sup. 1999) — Court held that "Work" in AIA waiver of subrogation clause included ALL work within the scope of the Agreement, whether the renovation affected new construction or the existing structure being renovated

### 22.2 The Catastrophe and Waiver of Subrogation
**Author:** William T. Kacerovskis, Esq.; Brown & James

**What happens when the project burns down:**
1. Owner's property insurer pays the claim
2. If waiver of subrogation clause exists → insurer is blocked from suing the GC, subs, or A/E — even if they caused it
3. If NO waiver → insurer can sue all parties; multiple lawsuits; project stalls for years

**Limitations of waiver of subrogation clauses:**
- Apply only to losses "covered by property insurance obtained pursuant to this contract"
- If no coverage (exclusion applies), waiver doesn't protect the contractor
- If loss exceeds coverage limits, owner can sue for the excess
- Missouri law (and similar jurisdictions): express waiver language required; implied waiver not recognized in construction

**Implied waiver of subrogation:** Some courts outside Missouri find implied waiver when contract requires owner to purchase property insurance; Missouri has not yet applied this to construction contracts

**Why waiver of subrogation clauses are valuable to OWNERS too:**
- Prevents the owner from being dragged into a multi-party liability fight while the project sits idle
- Allows reconstruction to begin immediately
- Preserves contractor and A/E relationships for the rebuild
- Eliminates attorney fees and delay costs that exceed the cost of the damage

> **BSIQ Application — Underwriting:**
> Waiver of subrogation is a critical gap in many smaller construction projects. BSIQ checklist items: (1) Does the owner's property policy have a blanket waiver of subrogation endorsement covering all project participants? (2) Does the GC's CGL policy have a waiver of subrogation endorsement? (3) Do all subcontracts contain reciprocal waiver language flowing down? Miss any one of these and a catastrophic loss becomes a litigation catastrophe. Content: "Why Every Construction Contract Needs a Waiver of Subrogation Clause — Even If You Trust Everyone."

## Chapter 23: Substantial Completion

### 23.1 When Is the Project Substantially Complete?
**Case:** *Kinetic Builders, Inc. v. F. Whitten Peters* (Fed. Cir. 2000, 226 F.3d 1307) — Government contracted for building with fire alarm system. System was installed but Air Force required full operational testing before occupancy — testing requirement was a government-imposed precondition that post-dated the contract. Court: this was governmental interference; contractor entitled to time extension. Substantial completion cannot be defeated by owner-imposed preconditions that were not part of the original contract scope.

**The doctrine of substantial completion:**
- "Only proper where a promisee has obtained, for all intents and purposes, the benefit it contracted for" (quoting the Board)
- BUT: "should not be carried to the extreme" — minor punch list items ≠ no substantial completion
- Owner must give contractor opportunity to correct known defects
- Contractor who "bargains for" a specific completion date/milestone has strong argument for substantial completion once that milestone is operationally achieved

> **BSIQ Application — Risk Scoring:**
> Substantial completion is the trigger for: (1) most warranty periods beginning; (2) retainage release; (3) responsibility for insurance shifting from builder's risk to permanent property; (4) punch list formation. BSIQ risk intake should confirm: Is substantial completion clearly defined in the contract? Who issues the certificate? Is the owner's project manager in agreement with the contractor on SC milestone? Ambiguity here = risk flag.

## Chapter 24: Surety

### 24.1 Surety Fails to Pay — Claim Barred by Statute of Limitations
**Case:** *United States v. American States Insurance Co.* (11th Cir. 2001) — Government's surety claim barred by six-year statute of limitations; contracting officer had issued final decision three years late. Once final decision issued, the six-year limitations clock had already run. Critical: the limitations period runs from when the government's claim against the surety ACCRUED, not when the contractor defaulted.

### 24.2 Miller Act Surety Requirements Amended (1999)
**Author:** Edward G. Gallagher, Esq.; Wickwire Gavin, P.C.

**Key changes from Construction Industry Payment Protection Act of 1999 (Pub.L. 106-49):**
- Payment bond raised from 3% of bid price to **100% of contract value**
- Written notice to prime contractor required within **90 days of last work** (registered mail no longer sufficient — written, personal delivery required)
- Suit must be filed within **1 year** of last work
- Arbitration clauses in subcontracts ARE enforceable (not voided by Miller Act)
- Waiver of Miller Act rights in subcontracts IS void

### 24.3 Surety Hit with Punitive Damages for Bad Faith
**Case:** *J&S Insulation, Inc. v. International Airport (Boston)* (Mass. Superior Court, SJC-09738, 2001) — Surety obtained a jury verdict of $220,000+ in punitive damages for bad faith failure to settle subcontractor's valid payment bond claim. Surety had settled with GC but refused sub's claim.

Massachusetts framework: (1) Chapter 176D (unfair settlement practices) and Chapter 93A (consumer protection) apply to sureties; (2) Sureties have statutory and common law obligations of good faith; (3) The bond claimant was a "beneficiary" of the surety's obligations — not just a third party.

### 24.4 Surety Market Update (Written ~2002)
**Author:** David C. Moylan; Marsh USA, Inc.

**2001 Industry Results:**
- Top 100 surety companies wrote ~$2.5 billion in premium
- Industry combined ratio: 142% (losing 42 cents per dollar)
- Second consecutive record loss year; Enron collapse was contributing factor
- 14 of top 25 surety companies had combined ratios >160%

**Impact on Contractors:**
1. Capacity restrictions for large accounts — multiple sureties now required for large projects
2. Environmental and financial guarantee capacity reduced/eliminated
3. No longer available for financially marginal contractors
4. Bond-loss guarantee programs eliminated for marginal firms

**Damage Control — What Contractors Must Do Now (still relevant):**
1. Approach surety renewal as critical — submit complete, quality packages
2. Discuss financial performance vs. industry peers; explain high/low points
3. Annual audits provided promptly (90 days); quarterly in-progress financials
4. Communicate frequently with broker and surety underwriter
5. Know your surety's reinsurance situation — some sureties using "captive" reinsurance
6. Maintain banking relationships; credit facilities are vital
7. Have your surety broker as your advisor — not just order taker

### 24.5 Bid Bonds on Federal Contracts — The Power of Attorney Problem
**Author:** Hal Perloff, Esq.; Wickwire Gavin

**The rule:** Bid bonds must include an ORIGINAL, CURRENT, properly signed power of attorney — photocopied, faxed, or mechanically-produced instruments are grounds for bid rejection as non-responsive.

| Case | Result |
|---|---|
| *Kemper Construction Co.* (Corps of Engineers) | Bid rejected — faxed power of attorney; GAO sustained Corps' rejection |
| *Scheppler Industries, Inc.* (GSBCA) | Same result — faxed document; consistently applied |

**Practice points:** (1) Always submit original power of attorney with bid bond; (2) Confirm the certification date on the power of attorney is current; (3) Never fax a bid bond package — deliver in person or use sealed bid process.

### 24.6 Default Insurance — An Alternative to Traditional Surety Bonds
**Author:** Terry Gray, Vice President; Construction Risk, Zurich North America Insurance

**What is Default Insurance (subcontractor default insurance / SDI)?**
- Developed by Zurich Insurance in 1995
- Purchased by the GENERAL CONTRACTOR to protect against defaults of subcontractors
- Unlike a surety bond (which protects the PROJECT OWNER), SDI protects the GC
- Covers: completion costs, correction costs for defective work, extended overhead, delay damages
- All subcontracts signed after policy inception are covered

**How it differs from surety bonds:**

| Factor | Surety Bond | Default Insurance |
|---|---|---|
| Who purchases | Each subcontractor | General contractor (master policy) |
| Who is protected | Project owner | General contractor |
| Control over default response | Surety | General contractor |
| Response speed | Slow (surety investigation) | Fast (GC controls) |
| Coverage breadth | Specific bond obligation | Broader (completion + correction + delay) |
| Pre-qualification | Surety's underwriting | GC's program (key to pricing) |

**GC's obligations under SDI:**
- Maintain an active, documented sub pre-qualification program
- Monitor financial and performance of covered subs throughout project
- Report potential defaults promptly
- Co-pay (deductible) typically applies per claim

**Why SDI market grew (from ~2000 to >$14B contract volume):**
- GCs frustrated with slow surety response to defaulting subs
- GCs preferred controlling sub replacement themselves
- For GCs with strong pre-qual programs, SDI was often more cost-effective

**Criticism from surety industry:** The surety provides an independent perspective; the GC may be emotionally invested in a troubled sub and delay declaring default; the implied duty of good faith creates different (and arguably better) risk management incentives under a surety bond.

> **BSIQ Application — Brokerage + Risk Scoring:**
> SDI is a niche but growing product that BSIQ brokerage can place for GC clients. Key to pricing: quality of the GC's sub pre-qualification program. BSIQ risk scoring question: Does GC have a formal documented sub pre-qual program? (yes = lower SDI premium). Content: "Subcontractor Default Insurance vs. Surety Bonds — Which One Actually Protects Your Project?"

## Chapter 25: Debarment and Suspension
**Author:** Simon Santiago, Esq.; Wickwire Gavin, P.C.

**What debarment/suspension means:**
- Contractor (and its affiliates) excluded from government contracting
- FAR 9.406-1(b) and 9.407-1(b): Government may debar/suspend affiliated entities
- Affiliation extends to: subsidiaries, parent companies, any entity where debarred contractor has ownership interest or "ability to control or influence management"
- Debarment is a BUSINESS DEATH SENTENCE for contractors dependent on government work

**The affiliation analysis:**
- Not necessarily based on degree of involvement — the mere STATUS of being affiliated with a suspended/debarred contractor can trigger action
- The government's power to suspend affiliates creates serious enterprise risk

**Practice principle:** Contractors should never provide agents with stacks of blank, original, pre-signed powers of attorney — this creates an appearance of impropriety that courts have upheld as grounds for bid rejection and potentially debarment.

> **BSIQ Application:**
> Debarment/suspension status check is a non-negotiable underwriting step for any federal contractor. BSIQ can automate a SAM.gov debarment check in the submission intake workflow. Content: "The Federal Contractor Mistake That Can Cost You All Government Work."

## Chapter 26: Termination of Contract

### 26.1 Contractor Terminated for Inadequate Response to Cure Notice
**Case:** *Richard Daning v. AEL Corporation* (Fed. Cir. 2000, 224 F.3d 1337) — Navy issued cure notice for schedule slippage. Contractor submitted a "short letter back" describing problems but without an adequate plan to cure. Navy terminated for default. Board initially sustained contractor's appeal. Federal Circuit reversed — the contractor's response did not adequately address all the problems raised in the cure notice.

**What a cure notice response MUST contain:**
1. Respond in writing within 10 days (government standard)
2. State ALL problems identified in the cure notice and address each
3. Describe specific actions being taken to correct performance
4. Provide a revised schedule showing realistic completion date
5. Demonstrate adequate resources (labor, equipment, financing) to complete

**Risk Management Note (from book):** The decision does not explain why the surety stood by. If this had been a surety-bonded job and the surety had taken an active role in assuring that contract plans were completed — or had arranged a takeover — the result would have been very different.

> **BSIQ Application — Content + Risk Scoring:**
> Cure notice response is one of the highest-stakes risk moments in a construction project. Content: "What to Do When You Receive a Cure Notice — You Have 10 Days to Save Your Contract." Risk scoring: Does GC have a documented escalation/response protocol for cure notices and non-performance situations?

## Chapter 27: Warranties

### 27.1 No Warranty by A/E to Third-Party Condominium Association
**Case:** *Advanced Design, Inc. v. Overture Condominium HOA* (Minn.) — A/E had no warranty obligation to condominium association (OCHA); no contract between them; Minnesota implied warranty provisions for home construction apply to builders, not architects; warranty "arises from the business of selling products rather than the business of architectural design services"

### 27.2 A/E Liability for Warranting Design on GMP Contract
**Case:** *Investment Properties, Inc. v. James B. Foster and Piasecki Construction Co.* (Vt.) — A/E represented it could design a building meeting owner's GMP; drew incomplete drawings representing them as ~90% complete when actually only ~50% complete; GMP exceeded by $1M+; court held A/E breached contract.

**The recovery rule:** Owner entitled to difference between what owner paid and what the project would have cost IF the design had been properly done initially — NOT a windfall for the owner based on the complete project value.

**Exception — "Special circumstances":** Where the owner would not have proceeded with the project at all (knowing the true cost), the court may award larger damages.

### 27.3 Engineer Not Liable for Breach of Implied Warranty on Road Design
**Case:** *K.B. Weygand & Associates v. Deerwood Lake Land Co.* (Ala. 2001) — Road subsided due to impermissibility of underlying soil; court held engineer could not be held to warranty standard when failure arose from a differing site condition outside the design scope. Engineers are not guarantors of their designs — they warrant professional effort and reasonable care.

### 27.4 Government Warranty of Performance-Type Specifications
- When government uses performance specifications, it warrants that compliance with those specs will achieve the intended result
- Government cannot later claim contractor is responsible for inadequacy of the performance spec the government drafted
- *Trataros Construction, Inc.* (GSBCA) — Government's performance spec for fiberglass panels was inadequate; contractor recovered additional costs when redesign was required

### 27.5 Do Contractor Warranties Ever Expire?
**Author:** Kristin L. Poppenberg, Esq.; Vargas & Helson, LLP

**Case:** *Koos v. Advanced Design, Inc.* (Minn. December 2001) — Dispute over when statutory homebuilder warranty ran; new home installed higher than specified, causing cracking and drainage problems; developer had tried to fix the problem in 1997; issue of whether June 1997 "fix" constituted a new breach triggering a new warranty period.

**Minnesota Statutes §§ 327A.01-.06 — Homebuilder Warranty Framework:**
- 1-year warranty from first occupancy: materials and workmanship defects
- 2-year warranty: major systems (plumbing, electrical, heating)
- 10-year warranty: major structural defects
- Builder warranty is MANDATORY — cannot be waived contractually
- Clock runs from "first occupancy" (first homeowner to occupy unit)

**Key principle on warranty expiration:** The breach date is when the defect MANIFESTS (or should have been discovered), NOT when the work was installed. A defect that first appears in year 9 of a 10-year structural warranty period is a new cause of action.

> **BSIQ Application — Risk Scoring + Content:**
> Statutory warranty periods vary by state and project type — residential construction often has mandatory warranties that cannot be disclaimed contractually. BSIQ risk scoring should include: project type (residential vs. commercial), state warranty law applicability, warranty duration in contract vs. statutory minimum. Content: "Homebuilder Warranties Don't Expire When You Think They Do — Here's the Real Legal Timeline."

## B. CGL Submission Underwriting Checklist (from this book)

7. **Federal projects:** Any operations at federal facilities? → Miller Act compliance required
