# Construction Disputes Under the Microscope — How Forensic Engineering Uncovers Why Failures Happen

**Source:** Lexology — HKA (Edward Poon)
**Date:** May 7, 2026

---

## Synthesis

HKA published a case study series demonstrating how forensic engineering overturns initial assumptions in construction disputes. Three cases are documented: (1) spontaneously shattering tempered glass panels in commercial towers — initial assumption blamed imported Chinese glass, but forensic investigation found domestically sourced glass failed at 3x the rate, and shared liability across five parties including the architect, engineer, GC, glazing sub, and owner; (2) water infiltration through a hotel's building envelope — attributed initially to defective roof membrane installation, but forensic analysis found multiple overlapping contributors including solar condensation, flashing deficiencies, and exterior cladding defects; (3) cracking ceramic tile floors in a multistory facility — third-party consultant blamed normal concrete shrinkage, but forensic investigation identified improper tile installation and a reinforcing steel deficiency as the actual causes.

The pattern across all three cases is consistent: early fault attribution by non-forensic parties anchored liability negotiations around a single cause and a single defendant. In each case, forensic engineering exposed systemic failures distributed across multiple parties and shifted the liability landscape materially. For risk managers and underwriters, this has a direct implication for how losses develop: initial claim reports and early expert opinions routinely misidentify root cause, which means reserve setting and subrogation strategy based on first-notice-of-loss information carries significant error risk.

The non-obvious implication: as construction delivery methods become more integrated (design-build, IPD, GMP), the traditional party-by-party liability structure breaks down. When the architect, engineer, GC, and major subs are operating under shared-risk contracts, forensic investigations increasingly find shared causation — but insurance policies are still written on a per-party basis with each party's defense interest adverse to the others. That adversarial insurance structure creates litigation incentives even in cases where a collaborative remediation approach would cost less. BSIQ's risk scoring could flag delivery method as a variable that predicts claims complexity, not just frequency — integrated delivery creates lower likelihood of small claims but higher complexity and duration when major failures occur.
