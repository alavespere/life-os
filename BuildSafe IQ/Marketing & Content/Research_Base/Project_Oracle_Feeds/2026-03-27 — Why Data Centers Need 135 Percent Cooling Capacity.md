# Why Your Data Center Needs 135% Cooling Capacity

**Source:** The Data Center Engineer — Christian Ruoff (interview with Craig MacFadyen, Strategy & Portfolio Management, Munters)
**Date:** March 27, 2026

---

## Synthesis

The emerging standard for hyperscale data center cooling design is 135% total capacity — approximately 95% from liquid cooling (direct-to-chip or immersion) plus 40% from supplemental air cooling (CRAHs). This intentional redundancy beyond 100% is not a design error; it is a deliberate hedge against a fundamental problem: cooling infrastructure must be designed and built years before the specific IT hardware it will serve is selected. Different liquid cooling approaches capture between 60% and 100% of heat load, and rack densities are climbing rapidly as AI workloads replace traditional compute. Designing both liquid and air systems with overlapping capacity means operators can shift cooling strategy as IT loads evolve without ripping out infrastructure. Craig MacFadyen of Munters (a leading cooling equipment manufacturer) notes that liquid cooling at hyperscale is "in its infancy" — the industry is only now deploying first-generation large-scale systems and won't have meaningful performance feedback data for 2–5 years.

For construction risk, this design complexity creates a distinct commissioning and handoff risk profile. A single pressurization error in a centralized CDU system, a BMS communications failure (MacFadyen identifies this as the most common real-world failure mode), or a cooling system designed for 95% liquid capture that receives hardware requiring only 60% capture — any of these mismatches can produce thermal events in a partially live facility. The handoff between builders' risk (which covers core and shell) and operational property coverage (which covers the IT equipment load) is the highest-risk transition point: at substantial completion, the building is live but the cooling system hasn't been proven against full AI workload. Builders' risk terminates precisely when the real operational risk is emerging.

The non-obvious implication: the 135% cooling design standard means that data center construction contracts are increasingly specifying MEP systems that are intentionally oversized relative to day-one operational loads. This creates a valuation and coverage ambiguity — what is the insurable value of a cooling system that is sized for a future load that may or may not materialize? For builders' risk underwriters, the PML modeling for cooling system losses needs to account for this oversizing, which traditional square-footage or replacement-cost-per-SF approaches miss entirely. BSIQ's risk scoring, if applied to data center construction typologies, needs a distinct MEP complexity dimension that doesn't exist in standard commercial construction risk models.
