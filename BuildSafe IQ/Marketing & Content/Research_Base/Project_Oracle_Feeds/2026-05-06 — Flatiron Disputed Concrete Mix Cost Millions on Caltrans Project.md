# Flatiron Said Disputed Concrete Mix Cost Millions on Large Caltrans Project

**Source:** Engineering News-Record — Elaine Silver & Richard Korman
**Date:** May 6, 2026
**Project:** Caltrans Fix 50 HOV Lane & Resurfacing, Sacramento — $511 million design-build, Flatiron Dragados West

---

## Synthesis

Flatiron Dragados West, the design-build contractor on Caltrans' $511 million Fix 50 project, had to demolish and rebuild parts of a concrete viaduct after discovering that concrete supplied by Sacramento-based Elite Ready Mix failed to meet the 6,000 psi compressive strength requirement. The root cause: Elite substituted Class N natural pozzolan for Class F fly ash — a contractually required cementitious material — without obtaining prior approval from Flatiron or Caltrans, citing fly ash supply chain disruptions caused by coal plant shutdowns. Flatiron filed a $21 million breach-of-contract lawsuit against Elite in January 2025; a settlement was reached in November 2025 on undisclosed terms. Caltrans confirmed the defective areas were repaired and the project remains on track for completion this summer.

This case illustrates a category of risk that is systematically underpriced in construction: approved-material substitution risk driven by supply chain pressure. The subcontractor made an engineering decision — substituting a different pozzolan — that seemed operationally reasonable given supply constraints but produced a material performance failure that required full demolition and rebuild. The contractor's design-build contract with Caltrans capped the amount of portland cement and required use of materials from an approved agency list, which meant Elite was operating under constrained material options that had never been stress-tested against supply chain volatility. The legal dispute cascaded into a three-party chain: Flatiron sued Elite, Elite cross-complained against its pozzolan supplier Golden State Pozzolan.

The non-obvious implication: design-build contracts that specify approved material lists create a hidden concentration risk — if one material on the approved list becomes unavailable, contractors face a choice between project delay and unauthorized substitution. Neither option is clean, and neither is priced into the original risk model. For BSIQ's risk scoring, material specification complexity and supply chain concentration for key structural inputs (fly ash, specialty aggregates, reinforcing steel alloys) should be treated as independent risk dimensions — not subsumed under generic "subcontractor risk." This is especially acute on public agency design-build work where material approval processes are slow and substitution windows are narrow.
