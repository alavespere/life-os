# High Court Rules War Zones Do Not Shield Contractors from Liability for Employee Misconduct

**Source:** Engineering News-Record — Pam McFarland
**Date:** April 24, 2026
**Case:** *Hencely v. Fluor Corp.*, U.S. Supreme Court, ruling released April 22, 2026
**Decision:** 6-3 (Thomas writing, joined by Sotomayor, Kagan, Gorsuch, Barrett, Jackson; Alito dissent joined by Roberts and Kavanaugh)

---

## Synthesis

The U.S. Supreme Court ruled 6-3 that military and construction contractors are not shielded from state tort claims when employee misconduct is alleged, even when operating in war zones. The case involved Winston Hencely, a U.S. service member permanently disabled in a 2016 suicide bomber attack carried out by Ahmad Nayeb — a local Afghan worker hired by Fluor Corp. under the military's "Afghan First" initiative. Fluor argued and two lower courts agreed that the Federal Tort Claims Act preempted the state tort claim and that military contractors operating under military command in war zones were constitutionally shielded. The Supreme Court rejected this, holding that the contractor's constitutional defense only applies when sued for conduct the federal government specifically requested — and the Army's own investigation found Fluor lacked clear oversight of Nayeb's reporting structure. The preemption doctrine invoked by the appeals court "lacks any foundation in the Constitution, federal statutes, or our precedents," the majority wrote.

This ruling narrows the federal preemption shield that defense contractors and government-contractor hybrid firms have relied on for over a decade. For construction and engineering firms operating in conflict zones, international development projects, or other environments where U.S. government contractor status might be invoked as a liability shield, the ruling signals that state tort exposure follows the contractor even in extreme operational environments — unless the specific conduct complained of was affirmatively directed by the federal government. The negligent hiring and supervision theory survived the constitutional challenge, which means workforce management practices — including screening, onboarding, oversight, and termination protocols for local or third-country nationals — now carry direct liability consequences.

The non-obvious implication: this ruling has an insurance dimension that isn't discussed in the article. Government contractor professional liability and E&O policies have historically been structured assuming broad preemption protection for warzone operations. The narrowing of that shield means policy language and coverage triggers for government contractor GL and employer's liability programs need to be re-examined for adequacy.
