## The Model: Johnny Harris

Johnny Harris is the primary style reference. Study his work before generating anything.

**What he does that we steal:**
- Opens with a question, anomaly, or hard fact that creates immediate tension
- Builds the story progressively — each paragraph earns the next
- Uses data as drama, not decoration
- Challenges the obvious narrative — the thing everyone assumes is wrong, or incomplete
- Speaks directly to camera (or reader) like a human, not a brand
- Never summarizes at the end with "so, in conclusion..." — he lands on a single sharp idea
- Short sentences when driving a point. Longer sentences when building context and pulling the reader deeper into the problem before the turn.
- No hedging. No "it could be argued that." State the thing.

**What we do NOT do:**
- "I'm excited to share..."
- "5 things you should know about..."
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- Bullet-point listicles as the primary format
- Corporate passive voice ("it has been determined that...")
- Fake humility ("I may be wrong but...")
- Engagement bait ("What do you think? Drop it in the comments!")

## Topic Ownership

Aaron is the definitive voice on **construction risk intelligence.** Every piece should reinforce one or more of these pillars:

1. **Risk is mispriced** — GCs, subs, and developers are paying for coverage that doesn't match their actual exposure (or vice versa)
2. **Data changes everything** — permit data, claims history, and project-level intelligence exist; most of the industry ignores them
3. **The insurance system is broken for construction** — wholesale, retail, surplus lines — the friction is real and fixable
4. **AI is the equalizer** — small firms can now access risk intelligence that was previously available only to the largest GCs
5. **Most risk events were predictable** — the accident, the claim, the project failure had signals. Here's what they were.

**What they care about:**

**What they do NOT care about:**
- Insurance theory
- Abstract AI discussions
- Content that talks down to them

## Structure — The Johnny Harris Framework (adapted)

Every piece — LinkedIn post, article, case study, video script — follows this spine:

```
1. THE HOOK      — One fact, anomaly, or question that creates tension
2. THE PROBLEM   — Why this matters. What's broken, misunderstood, or invisible
3. THE EVIDENCE  — Data, case, or example that proves the problem is real
4. THE TURN      — The thing most people get wrong, or the non-obvious truth
5. THE LANDING   — One sharp idea. What to do or think differently. No "in conclusion."
```

## Format-Specific Rules

### LinkedIn Posts

**Short format (150–300 words):**
- First line is the hook — no context-setting, no warmup
- No more than 3 lines before a line break
- Maximum 1 emoji — only if it genuinely adds signal (not decoration)
- End on a provocative statement or hard question — not a CTA
- Never ask for likes or shares

**Long format (400–700 words):**
- Same hook rule
- Use white space aggressively — walls of text get skipped
- One idea per post. Not five. One.
- If it needs a list, the list earns its place through contrast or data — not as a crutch

---

### Long-Form Articles

- Minimum 800 words, target 1,200–2,000
- Thesis stated by paragraph 2 — not at the end
- Every section advances the argument — no filler sections
- Use real numbers, real project types, real claim scenarios (anonymized if needed)
- End with a hard landing — one sentence that is the entire point of the piece

---

### Case Studies

- Lead with the outcome, not the background ("A GC walked away from a $2.1M claim with $0 out of pocket. Here's what they did differently.")
- Structure: Situation → Risk Signal → Decision → Outcome → Lesson
- Anonymize if needed but keep the numbers real
- The lesson must be actionable — not "work with a good broker"

---

### Video Scripts (Johnny Harris Style)

- Open with visual + voice — describe the scene before the argument
- Pacing: short punchy lines for impact beats, longer lines for context building
- Use "here's the thing" / "but here's what nobody talks about" type pivots sparingly and only when earned
- Every script has one thesis. One. The video is built to prove that thesis.
- End on the idea, not on a channel plug

## What Good Looks Like — Test Before Publishing

Before any piece goes out, run it through these checks:

- [ ] Does the first line create tension or curiosity without context?
- [ ] Is there one clear thesis, stated or strongly implied?
- [ ] Does every paragraph earn the next one?
- [ ] Is there at least one data point, case, or specific detail that makes this real?
- [ ] Does it end on a sharp landing — not a summary, not a CTA?
- [ ] Would a busy GC read past the first paragraph?
- [ ] Does it sound like Aaron, or does it sound like a marketing team?
