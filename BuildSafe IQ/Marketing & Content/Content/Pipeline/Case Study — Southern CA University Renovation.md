# Case Study — Southern California University Renovation
_Status: Published example | Format: Case Study | Last updated: 2026-04-06_

---

**The outcome first:**
A public university in Southern California handed a renovation contract to the lowest bidder. Three months after the scheduled completion date, students are returning to a construction zone. The taxpayers funding a 10-year municipal bond are now watching that money disappear into a project no one can stop.

Here's what went wrong — and what was predictable from day one.

---

## The Situation
A regional public university initiated a campus renovation funded by a 10-year municipal improvement bond. The project had a hard completion deadline: before students returned in the fall.

Public bidding law required awarding the contract to the lowest bidder. The winning firm was a local contractor with a documented history of schedule delays.

The contract had no liquidated damages clause.

---

## The Risk Signal
Three signals were present before a single shovel hit the ground:

1. **Contractor track record:** The winning firm's history of schedule delays was publicly documented — and ignored because the law required lowest bid selection.
2. **No financial consequence for delay:** A contract without liquidated damages removes the primary enforcement mechanism for schedule adherence.
3. **No central authority on workflow:** No mechanism was established to ensure the GC — not the subcontractors — owned the project schedule.

Any one of these flags, identified at the contracting phase, warranted mitigation. All three appeared simultaneously.

---

## What Happened
- Subcontractors dictated work sequencing rather than the general contractor
- No central coordination of trades, inspections, or critical path items
- High rate of rework: quality control failures led to repeated tear-outs
- No liquidated damages = no financial consequence for the contractor
- Project delayed from May completion to late August — directly into student move-in

---

## The Outcome
Students returned to a construction zone. The bond — designed to fund multiple improvement projects over a decade — now risks being partially consumed by cost overruns from a single project. Future campus upgrades are in jeopardy.

The contractor faces zero financial consequences.

---

## The Lesson
**Lowest bid is not lowest cost.** The risk premium of a contractor with a poor track record is real — it just gets paid by the client, not the contractor.

Three things would have changed this outcome:
1. **Contractor vetting weighted by track record,** not just price
2. **Liquidated damages clause** with meaningful per-day penalties tied to the bond timeline
3. **A GC held accountable for schedule,** not individual subcontractors managing their own sequencing

Risk mitigation doesn't start at the first sign of a problem. It starts at the contract.

---

## The Single Idea
Every delayed public project isn't a construction failure. It's a procurement failure — and the taxpayers who funded the bond pay the difference.
