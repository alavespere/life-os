## The Data That Won't Go Away

91.5% of major construction projects fail to finish on time or within budget.

That's not a recent finding. That's from a study of 16,000 global projects — and the number hasn't moved in decades. Only 0.5% of projects finish on time, on budget, and within scope.

The average construction dispute now drags on for 14–15 months, costing over $42.8M per case. 2025 figures are expected to exceed $43.1M as inflation and risk pricing continue to climb.

Meanwhile, powerful platforms like Procore and Autodesk sit largely unused above the field level. The C-suite and risk managers aren't in the system. Stakeholders operate in silos. Rework, scheduling delays, and finger-pointing remain the norm.

---

## Why the Pattern Persists

The construction industry has an inconvenient truth: the broken system is profitable — for some of the people inside it.

- Large GCs buy tools but don't use the data to guide decisions, because the wrong people hold the controls
- Insurance premiums rose 4.6% in 2024–2025, driven by higher litigation exposure
- Some projects in high-risk markets like Florida are going completely uninsured
- Outdated insurance terms are still being used in contracts — the CG 2010 11/85 endorsement hasn't applied in over four decades
- Public policy and regulation have done little to rebalance power in high-litigation jurisdictions like New York and California

---

## The Root Problem

At the core of it is a delivery model that sets the contractor and engineer against each other from day one.

The contract is written to assign blame, not to align incentives. The bid process rewards the lowest number, not the most realistic one. The insurance structure protects against loss rather than preventing it.

Until the incentive structure changes, the pattern won't.

---

## What's Actually Different Now

Three things have changed in the last five years that didn't exist before:

1. **Live data at the project level** — Procore, Autodesk, and similar platforms capture more project data than any previous generation of tools. Most of it sits unanalyzed.

2. **AI that can find patterns humans miss** — The signals that predict cost overruns, subcontractor defaults, and schedule failures exist in the data. They're just not being read.

3. **A financial model that aligns incentives** — Risk management services tied to measurable outcomes (TCOR reduction, schedule performance) create accountability that pure insurance placement never has.

## The Landing
*(Needs sharpening — this is the thesis)*

The construction industry doesn't have a technology problem. It has a data-use problem. The tools exist. The data exists. What's missing is someone whose job it is to read the signals before the project goes sideways — and who has enough skin in the game to care.
